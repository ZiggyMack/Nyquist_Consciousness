Okay, let's take a look at the likely causes of DDR3 address or command ringback oscillation:

Ringback oscillation in DDR3 signals is often caused by improper signal termination and transmission line effects. Here are the key factors to consider:

Termination: Proper termination is critical for DDR3 signals to prevent reflections and ringing. The address and command lines should be terminated at both ends with the correct impedance, typically 50-60Î©. Mismatched termination can lead to reflections and oscillations.

Topology: The routing topology of the DDR3 traces can also contribute to ringing. Stubs, bends, and other discontinuities in the transmission line can cause impedance changes that generate reflections. The layout should minimize these kinds of discontinuities.

Via stubs: Vias connecting the top and bottom PCB layers can act as stubs and cause reflections if not properly designed. Via stubs should be minimized in length and their impedance matched to the trace impedance.

Other factors like driver strength, board material, and layer stackup can also impact signal integrity and contribute to ringing. An experienced hardware engineer would need to carefully analyze the full DDR3 interface design to identify the root causes.
