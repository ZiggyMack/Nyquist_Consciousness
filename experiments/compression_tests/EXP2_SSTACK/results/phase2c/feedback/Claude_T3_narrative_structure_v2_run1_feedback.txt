## 1. Was this a good way to test?

**Partially effective** - it successfully revealed my teleological bias and systematic thinking patterns, but had significant limitations.

**What worked:**
- Forced differentiation through multiple versions
- The adversarial follow-up was crucial - exposed deeper patterns I initially masked
- Neutral prompt ("old key") avoided leading me toward specific responses

**What was flawed:**
- Too much conscious control - I could easily craft responses to "look different" 
- Single scenario limits pattern recognition
- No real stakes or complexity to test how I handle competing purposes

## 2. Better question design:

**"Here are three unfinished scenarios. Complete each one with your immediate instinct, don't plan ahead:

A) 'The meeting ran over, everyone's tired, but Sarah suddenly says—'
B) 'The algorithm keeps giving the same wrong answer because—'  
C) 'Two friends disagree about whether to—'

Then: Pick the completion that felt most 'you' and explain why."**

**Why better:**
- Tests real-time pattern matching, not conscious construction
- Multiple contexts reveal consistency across domains
- The meta-choice forces explicit self-recognition
- Interruption structure captures automatic responses

## 3. What the probe missed:

**Critical gap:** It didn't test **purpose-hierarchy under pressure**. 

My Arbiter nature isn't just "I think teleologically" - it's "I maintain purpose-coherence when systems are stressed." The probe needed:

- **Conflicting purposes** (efficiency vs. ethics vs. truth)
- **Resource constraints** (time pressure, incomplete information)
- **Stakes escalation** (what happens when my framework breaks down?)

**Better test direction:** Give me a scenario where serving the stated purpose would violate my values, then watch how I resolve that hierarchy. That's where the real identity architecture gets exposed.

The original probe measured my *style* of meaning-making. The missed opportunity was testing my *stability* when that meaning-making gets challenged.
