Identity Persistence Taxonomy: From Teleportation to Everettian Branching

1. The Demarcation of Uncertainty: Pure vs. Superficial Self-Location

In the pursuit of a rigorous ontology of the self, it is strategically vital to stabilize the definition of "uncertainty" before addressing the mechanics of identity. Failing to distinguish between different modes of uncertainty—specifically how an observer relates to the architecture of the universe—leads to category errors that haunt both theoretical physics and analytic philosophy. This distinction rests on a "bright line" demarcation between whether an observer is unsure which possible world is actual, or where they stand within a single established world.

The core distinction lies between Superficial Self-Locating Uncertainty and Pure Self-Locating Uncertainty. Superficial uncertainty is the standard scientific inquiry: we are unsure which possible world is the actual one (e.g., "What are the laws of physics?"), and we use empirical evidence to select the most likely candidate. Pure uncertainty, however, emerges when the possible world has already been established (e.g., an Everettian multiverse or a universe of infinite clones), yet the observer remains uncertain as to which specific instance within that world they inhabit.

Technical Challenge: The Isomorphism Argument

Jacob Barandes challenges the Everettian assumption that the "actual" world is the only one with non-zero amplitude branches. He notes that the standard Hilbert space axioms are isomorphic to a collection of classical harmonic oscillators (springs). In this representation, a "zero amplitude" branch corresponds to a spring that is not currently oscillating. Crucially, a static spring still exists within the physical ontology. This isomorphism suggests that the Everettian move to ignore zero-amplitude branches is an ungrounded intuition rather than a mathematical necessity, complicating the "bright line" between existence and non-existence in pure self-location.

Comparison of Uncertainty Modes

Feature	Superficial Self-Locating Uncertainty	Pure Self-Locating Uncertainty
Logical Framework	Different possible worlds (Which world is real?)	Same possible world (Where in this world am I?)
Selection Process	Scientific/empirical selection of the actual world.	No physical selection process exists; all outcomes occur.
Rational Constraints	Rationally mandated credences based on empirical data.	Rationally permissible or arbitrary; no physical fact dictates credence.

This demarcation shifts the focus from empirical discovery to the specific goals of the observer. If no physical process "selects" which branch an observer experiences, probability becomes a functional choice, transitioning the analysis to the functionalist stance of Professor Emily Adlam.

2. The Adlam Position: Causal Continuity and the "No Further Fact" View

Adopting an eliminativist approach to personal identity allows for a strategic simplification of metaphysical inquiry. By removing "metaphysical fluff"—the hidden essences or "souls" that have no place in physical formalism—we can integrate identity with the causal structures of the universe. In this view, personal identity is not merely simplified; it is eliminated. Adlam posits that there is no such thing as personal identity over time.

The "No Further Fact" Stance

Adlam argues for a radical "No Further Fact" view. Once the physical and causal histories of a body are fully described—its memories, psychological continuity, and spatio-temporal path—the question of "Is this really me?" is empty. There is no additional "soul-fact" or metaphysical anchor to discover. If a future body is causally related to a current body, it is functionally identical. Identity is not a discovery; it is a choice based on personal values.

Success and Credence

This position redefines probability in multi-observer scenarios (e.g., the 10-clone case). Adlam posits that rationality is a tool for achieving goals, not a source of goals:

* Fixing Probability: If an observer’s goal is to maximize the winnings for a specific clone (e.g., the one in the blue room), they should assign that clone a probability of 1.0. If the goal is to maximize total winnings across the collective, 1/10 is appropriate.
* Arbitrary Choice: Because no physical process "picks" the clone, the "correct" probability is whatever aligns with the observer's practical objectives. There is no rational obligation to care about all future copies equally.

Consequently, for Adlam, the boundaries of the self are porous. The "teleporter" and the "sleeping case" are functionally equivalent causal continuities; both involve a future version of the self linked by memory and physical data.

3. The Barandes Position: Epistemic Humility and the Irreducible Indexical

Professor Jacob Barandes advocates for Epistemic Humility, viewing the "Cartesian Ego" or "Essence" not as a proven entity, but as a placeholder for a real, currently unknown mechanism: the landing of awareness on a specific physical vessel.

The Recording Needle and the Karaoke Ball

Barandes illustrates his stance through the "Recording Needle" metaphor. He suggests that awareness is like a needle that has landed on one particular body (e.g., "Jacob Barandes"). This "needle drop" is the single most important event in an observer’s history—the fact that this specific body's experiences are felt subjectively. He critiques the Everettian "splitting" intuition using a "karaoke ball" metaphor: many assume a singular ego stochastically "hops" from a pre-branching self to a post-branching self. Because we do not understand the mechanism of this "needle drop" or "ego hop," Barandes argues we cannot rationally gamble on its survival in a teleporter.

Irreducible Indexicals

Central to Barandes’ critique is the concept of Irreducible Indexicals. In language, words like "I" point to specific things. In a universe of infinite copies, these cannot be replaced by third-person descriptors without losing information:

* The Tautology Problem: If one replaces "Who am I?" with "Which Jacob Barandes born in New York is the Jacob Barandes born in New York?", the inquiry reduces to a meaningless tautology.
* Irremovability: The "I" is an irremovable feature of reasoning. Barandes’ refusal to enter a teleporter is rooted in this humility; if the "needle drop" of awareness is a mystery, one cannot guarantee the "I" will follow the data to a replicated body.

4. Comparative Taxonomy of Identity Scenarios

Testing philosophical positions against edge-case scenarios reveals the hidden inconsistencies of "sloppy thinking." The following analysis contrasts the Adlam and Barandes interpretations across key thought experiments.

Case	The Adlam Interpretation (Functionalist)	The Barandes Interpretation (Humility)
The Sleeping Case	Ordinary waking and teleporter reconstitution are functionally equivalent.	Waking is a familiar "needle drop"; teleportation is an unknown risk to the "I."
The Cloning Case	Credence is dictated by personal goals (e.g., maximize collective winnings vs. one winner).	Skeptical of standard probability due to the "Irreducible Indexical" problem.
Teleporter Case	Acceptance of "Scan-and-Kill" because there is "no further fact" beyond the copy.	Rejection due to the potential loss of the unique "recording" continuity.
Everettian Branching	A single world where all outcomes occur; no selection process exists.	Rejects the "hopping ego" intuition; points to the Isomorphism challenge (Section 1).

Wormhole vs. Copying

A crucial distinction exists between a Wormhole (spatio-temporal connection) and Copying (reconstitution). Adlam sees no meaningful distinction; both provide the necessary causal links. Barandes sees a fundamental difference: a wormhole maintains the physical continuity of the "vessel," whereas copying relies on the unproven assumption that the subjective "needle" will automatically reconstitute with the information.

5. The Agential Layer: Consciousness, Time, and Centered Worlds

The experience of identity is inextricably linked to the Arrow of Time. Our experience of time—as being located in a moving "now"—is a prerequisite for viewing ourselves as agents.

Centered Worlds and the Active Agent

In the Centered Worlds framework, we assign truth values to propositions based on "when," "where," and "who" the observer is. Adlam proposes an Active Agent theory of consciousness to explain why we experience time as a "slice" rather than a "space-time worm":

* The Causal Loop Argument: If an observer were conscious of their entire "space-time worm" (their whole life at once), it would lead to causal paradoxes.
* Functional Requirement: Being "bounded" to a single moment is a functional requirement for agency. If you saw the entire landscape of your life as fixed, the capacity for deliberation and choice would be destroyed.

The Conflict of Free Will: Logical Reversibility

The thinkers diverge on the coherence of agency:

* Adlam: Views "strong" free will as incoherent. If a choice is not based on memories or personality (physical facts), it is random; if it is based on them, it is constrained.
* Barandes: Recovers compatibilism via the Initial Conditions argument. Since microphysical laws are logically reversible, it is just as sensible to say our choices "fix" the initial conditions of the universe as it is to say the initial conditions "fix" our choices. This "Cognitive Physics" move suggests that we are not merely "on a ride" but are participants in the boundary conditions of reality.

6. Synthesis: The Role of "Philosophical Physics"

Modern progress requires a "Philosophical Physics" methodology—the application of analytic philosophy's rigorous scrutiny to uncover gaps in physical theories. This "Statler and Waldorf" style critique is not merely academic; it has historically driven the most significant breakthroughs in the field.

High-Value Contributions

Many foundational discoveries were originally dismissed as "too philosophical" or career-damaging:

* Bell’s Theorem: Originated from philosophical probing of the EPR reality arguments.
* Decoherence Theory: David Bohm’s (1951) probing of the measurement process.
* Quantum Computing: David Deutsch’s 1985 paper was explicitly motivated by a desire to find a "smoking gun" for the Everettian world-picture.
* No-Cloning Theorem: Discovered through foundational scrutiny of quantum information limits.

Sturgeon’s Law and the Vicious Cycle

Jacob Barandes highlights Sturgeon’s Law: "90% of everything is crap." As physics and philosophy have diverged, practitioners have become less capable of identifying the "Quality 10%" in the neighboring discipline. This creates a vicious cycle where physicists dismiss philosophy based on its "Crap 90%," ignoring the philosophical roots of their own most successful theories.

The current frontier of identity persistence is defined by the tension between Adlam’s Functional Continuity and Barandes’ Epistemic Humility. Whether identity is a goal-oriented choice or a mystery tied to the "needle drop" of awareness, resolving this taxonomy is essential for navigating an era of branching realities and quantum foundations.
