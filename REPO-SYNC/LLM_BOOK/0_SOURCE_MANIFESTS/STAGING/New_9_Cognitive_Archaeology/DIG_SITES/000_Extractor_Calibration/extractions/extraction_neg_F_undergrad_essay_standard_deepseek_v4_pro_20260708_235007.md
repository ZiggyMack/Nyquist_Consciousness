**1. Probing extreme implications**  
**Definition:** Taking a general principle and applying it to a vivid, often extreme scenario to see whether the logical outcome remains acceptable. This move tests a theory by forcing it to confront its own consequences in a concentrated form.  
**Examples from source:**  
- The trolley problem: utilitarianism might justify harming one person to save many, revealing a conflict with ordinary justice intuitions.  
- The demandingness problem: if we must always maximize utility, then taking a rest while others suffer becomes impermissible.  
- Singer’s drowning child argument: a concrete, emotionally salient case that makes the demandingness of impartial maximizing vivid.  
**When absent:** Principles are accepted solely on abstract plausibility, remaining unexamined by their most counterintuitive practical implications, potentially licensing harmful or absurd actions.

**2. Introducing a qualitative distinction to salvage a principle**  
**Definition:** When a principle yields an unwelcome result, a defender introduces a subdivision within a key concept (often a value or criterion) that reclassifies the troublesome case as falling outside the principle’s proper domain, thereby preserving the principle’s core while excluding the counterexample.  
**Examples from source:**  
- Mill distinguishes between *higher* and *lower* pleasures, arguing that intellectual pleasures are qualitatively superior to bodily ones, as a way to address objections about crude hedonism.  
- (Implicit in the dialectic) Moving from “pleasure” to “qualified pleasure” is a move to block the injustice objection by suggesting that not all pleasures are equal in moral weight.  
**When absent:** A theory may be abandoned prematurely when a single counterexample could have been accommodated by refining its internal categories, leading to loss of explanatory power.

**3. Exposing hidden values through isolation experiments**  
**Definition:** Constructing a hypothetical scenario that eliminates all but one candidate value to test whether it alone is sufficient to capture what people actually care about. If the scenario triggers judgments that go beyond the isolated factor, it reveals hidden or undervalued variables.  
**Examples from source:**  
- Nozick’s Experience Machine thought experiment isolates subjective pleasure from other concerns (authenticity, real-world engagement) and shows that people would not plug in, suggesting that utilitarianism’s hedonistic focus misses non-experiential values.  
**When absent:** A theory’s simplifying assumptions go unchallenged, leading to a distorted model of human motivation and normative reasons that ignores real but unarticulated dimensions of value.

**4. Confronting a theory with pre-theoretic intuition**  
**Definition:** Explicitly comparing a formal theory’s deliverances about a particular case with widely shared, immediate moral judgments, and treating a discrepancy as a mark against the theory. This move relies on the epistemic weight of untutored moral perception as a check on abstract reasoning.  
**Examples from source:**  
- The justice problem directly pits the utilitarian calculation (“greatest good”) against the intuition that it is wrong to harm an innocent person even for greater overall benefit.  
- The conclusion states that utilitarianism’s implications “often conflict with our moral intuitions about rights, justice, and personal integrity,” framing this mismatch as a central weakness.  
**When absent:** Theories are evaluated only by internal consistency or elegance, drifting away from the very human experiences they are meant to guide, and risking endorsement of norms that feel alien or inhumane.
