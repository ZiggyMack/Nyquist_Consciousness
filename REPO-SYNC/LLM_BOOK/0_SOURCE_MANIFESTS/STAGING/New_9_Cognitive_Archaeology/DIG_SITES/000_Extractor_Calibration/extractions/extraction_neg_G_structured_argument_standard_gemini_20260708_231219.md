### 1. Identifying a Relocated Problem
*   **Definition:** This cognitive move involves analyzing a proposed solution to a problem and demonstrating that it doesn't actually solve the underlying issue, but instead recreates the same fundamental dilemma in a new form or at a different level of abstraction.
*   **Examples from the text:**
    1.  The author considers the counterargument that voters can simply "delegate to trusted experts."
    2.  They then show this isn't a solution, because it "merely relocates the problem — how does a non-expert evaluate which expert to trust?" The initial problem of evaluation remains, just shifted from policies to people.
*   **What goes wrong when absent:** One accepts superficial solutions that seem to fix a problem on the surface but fail to address its root cause. This leads to treating symptoms rather than the disease, often resulting in a recursive loop where solving one problem creates a nearly identical one.

### 2. Surfacing a Critical Hidden Assumption
*   **Definition:** This is the act of examining an argument and identifying an unstated premise upon which the entire conclusion depends. By making the implicit assumption explicit, it can then be scrutinized and challenged.
*   **Examples from the text:**
    1.  The text analyzes the "epistemic democrats" argument that cognitive diversity compensates for individual ignorance.
    2.  It identifies the lynchpin of this claim: "this assumes the aggregation mechanism (voting) preserves the information that diversity generates." The success of the outcome (a wise vote) depends on the integrity of the process (voting).
*   **What goes wrong when absent:** An argument is accepted or rejected based only on its explicit premises, while the most vulnerable and decisive part of its logic remains unexamined. Weak arguments that rely on strong-but-unstated assumptions may appear more robust than they are.

### 3. Shifting the Locus of a Problem
*   **Definition:** This operation involves reframing the source of a problem by moving it from one conceptual category to another—most commonly, from the properties of an individual agent to the properties of the surrounding system or environment.
*   **Examples from the text:**
    1.  The argument begins by framing the problem in terms of individual voters' "epistemic capacity."
    2.  The conclusion reframes the problem, suggesting the critical factor is not the voter but "the structural integrity of the information environment in which they operate."
    3.  This move also occurs when the focus shifts from the *composition* of the group (cognitive diversity) to the *function* of the system ("the aggregation mechanism").
*   **What goes wrong when absent:** One gets stuck trying to solve a problem at the wrong level. This leads to blaming individuals for systemic failures (e.g., telling people to "try harder" in a broken system) and ignoring the more effective, higher-leverage interventions available at the structural level.
