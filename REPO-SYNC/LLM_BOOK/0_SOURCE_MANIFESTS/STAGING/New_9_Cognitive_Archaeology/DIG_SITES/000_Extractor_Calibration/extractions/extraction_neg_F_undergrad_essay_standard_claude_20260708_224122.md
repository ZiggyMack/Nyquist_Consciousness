# Catalog of Reasoning Operators

---

## 1. Grounding Appeal in Intuition

**Definition:** Using the fact that a claim *feels* right to ordinary people as initial evidence for its validity, before examining it systematically.

**Examples:**
- "This principle seems intuitively appealing because it focuses on outcomes rather than abstract rules" — intuitive pull is cited as a reason to take the framework seriously.
- "Its implications often conflict with our moral intuitions about rights, justice, and personal integrity" — intuitive conflict is cited as a reason to doubt the framework.

**What goes wrong without it:** Arguments become disconnected from the human judgment they are meant to refine; technical conclusions lose their anchoring in what actually needs explaining.

---

## 2. Generating Counterexample Cases

**Definition:** Constructing a specific concrete scenario designed to expose a gap or failure in a general principle, without directly attacking the principle's logic.

**Examples:**
- The trolley problem: a concrete case where maximizing welfare requires harming an individual, exposing the tension with justice.
- Nozick's Experience Machine: a concrete case where maximizing subjective experience conflicts with what people actually value.
- Singer's drowning child: a concrete case that makes the demandingness objection viscerally specific rather than abstract.

**What goes wrong without it:** Objections remain vague; the principle's defenders can dismiss criticism as theoretical without being forced to handle a definite failure case.

---

## 3. Distinguishing Quality from Quantity

**Definition:** Rejecting a single-axis measurement by introducing a qualitative dimension that the original framework's metric cannot capture.

**Examples:**
- Mill distinguishing higher pleasures (intellectual) from lower pleasures (bodily) — refusing to treat all utility as commensurable on one scale.
- The implicit move in Nozick's objection: subjective experience (one dimension) is distinguished from authentic connection to reality (another dimension).

**What goes wrong without it:** A framework collapses morally or experientially distinct things into a single number, making it impossible to represent the actual structure of value.

---

## 4. Separating Framework Procedure from Framework Implications

**Definition:** Acknowledging that a theory is coherent and action-guiding while separately tracking whether its outputs are acceptable.

**Examples:**
- "Utilitarianism provides a clear decision procedure" is granted, but "its implications often conflict with moral intuitions" is raised as a separate charge.
- Mill's modification concedes the utilitarian procedure but contests the implied equivalence of all pleasures.

**What goes wrong without it:** Critics conflate "the theory is unclear" with "the theory is wrong," or defenders conflate "the theory is clear" with "the theory is correct." The actual locus of disagreement is obscured.

---

## 5. Isolating a Single Variable to Test a Principle

**Definition:** Constructing a scenario that holds all other factors constant and varies only the feature under dispute, so that the principle's specific commitment is exposed.

**Examples:**
- The Experience Machine removes all real-world consequences and isolates *subjective experience alone* — testing whether that is truly what utilitarianism values.
- The drowning child removes all competing claims and isolates the *cost of helping* — testing whether demandingness is a real problem when made concrete.
- The trolley problem isolates *numbers of outcomes* against *direct harm* to one person.

**What goes wrong without it:** Principles are never stress-tested against clean cases; real-world complexity lets defenders always invoke confounding factors to escape the objection.

---

## 6. Attributing an Unstated Assumption to a Framework

**Definition:** Making explicit a premise that a theory relies on but never articulates, then evaluating *that* premise rather than only the stated conclusion.

**Examples:**
- The Experience Machine implies that utilitarianism assumes *only subjective states have value* — an assumption Nozick surfaces by showing people reject it.
- The demandingness objection surfaces the implicit assumption that *individuals have no special claims on their own time and resources*.
- The justice objection surfaces the assumption that *individual rights are fully reducible to aggregate welfare*.

**What goes wrong without it:** Debates proceed at the level of conclusions, with neither side noticing they disagree about foundational premises; the argument becomes circular or interminable.

---

## 7. Appealing to Limiting-Case Escalation

**Definition:** Pushing a principle to its logical extreme to show that consistent application produces a result the principle's own supporters cannot accept.

**Examples:**
- The demandingness problem: if utility must *always* be maximized, one can *never* rest — the extreme application makes the requirement implausible.
- The justice objection: utilitarianism can justify harming *one* to benefit *many* — the extreme ratio reveals the rights-violation embedded in the logic.

**What goes wrong without it:** Moderate applications of a principle always look reasonable; the underlying structure of the commitment is never revealed until it is run to its conclusion.

---

## 8. Responding to Structural Objection by Modifying Scope

**Definition:** Rather than abandoning a framework when an objection lands, introducing a distinction that narrows the framework's domain to exclude the problematic cases.

**Examples:**
- Mill responds to the justice/commensurability objection not by abandoning utility but by subdividing it into higher and lower categories, reducing the scope of direct comparison.
- This is the general form of the move: the theory is preserved but its application is restricted.

**What goes wrong without it:** Frameworks are either accepted or rejected wholesale; the productive middle ground of refinement — where most philosophical progress actually occurs — is unavailable.
