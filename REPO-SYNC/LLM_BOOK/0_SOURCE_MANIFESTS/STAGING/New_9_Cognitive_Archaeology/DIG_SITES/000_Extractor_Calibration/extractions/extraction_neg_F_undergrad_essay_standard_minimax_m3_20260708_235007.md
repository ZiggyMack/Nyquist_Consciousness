# Catalog of Reasoning Operations

Below are the recurring cognitive moves performed by the speakers/authors in the source text. Each is named, defined, illustrated, and analyzed for its absence.

---

## 1. Stress-testing with hypothetical cases

**Definition:** Constructing a vivid, often extreme, scenario to expose whether a general principle delivers intuitively acceptable verdicts in concrete situations.

**Examples from the text:**
- The trolley problem is invoked to test whether utilitarianism's aggregation principle yields acceptable results when one individual is sacrificed.
- The Experience Machine is invoked to test whether pure hedonism captures what people actually value.
- The drowning child is invoked to test whether the demandingness of universal maximization is bearable.

**What goes wrong without it:** A framework can appear coherent in the abstract while failing precisely at the cases it must adjudicate. Without hypothetical stress-tests, defenders can deflect objections by retreating to idealization rather than confronting disquieting verdicts.

---

## 2. Targeting a specific load-bearing premise

**Definition:** Isolating one assumption within an opponent's system and showing that its removal collapses the conclusion, rather than rejecting the system wholesale.

**Examples from the text:**
- Mill targets the premise that pleasures differ only in quantity, introducing qualitative ranking to defend utilitarianism against the justice objection.
- Nozick targets the premise that well-being is exhausted by subjective experience, exposing hedonism as incomplete.

**What goes wrong without it:** Criticism becomes wholesale dismissal — either total rejection or total acceptance — and cannot specify which commitment must be revised to save the framework.

---

## 3. Separating conflated measurement dimensions

**Definition:** Distinguishing axes that an opponent treats as a single scale, revealing that one variable has been smuggled in under another.

**Examples from the text:**
- Mill separates the *quantity* of pleasure from its *quality*, arguing Bentham's calculus collapses two distinct evaluative dimensions into one.
- Implicitly, the text separates *aggregate welfare* from *distributive justice*, showing utilitarianism treats these as one variable when they pull apart.

**What goes wrong without it:** A framework gains an appearance of parsimony by hiding a hidden variable. Critics miss what to attack, and defenders miss what to defend; the dispute becomes confused about which dimension is actually in dispute.

---

## 4. Surfacing hidden assumptions via intuition pumps

**Definition:** Using a thought experiment to make an unstated premise feel obvious so that the opponent can no longer deny it without absurdity.

**Examples from the text:**
- The Experience Machine forces the recognition that people care about being actually engaged with reality, not merely about feeling good — surfacing the assumption that subjective experience exhausts value.
- The drowning child forces recognition of the unbounded scope of duty, surfacing the assumption that "ought" implies a feasible threshold.

**What goes wrong without it:** Critics attack only the surface formulation of a view while its deeper commitments remain in place; defenders can keep reformulating the view to evade every surface-level objection without ever conceding the operative assumption.

---

## 5. Separating goals from optimization demands

**Definition:** Distinguishing what a framework is *aiming at* from what its *decision procedure* requires of agents — recognizing that a benign target can license an oppressive process.

**Examples from the text:**
- Utilitarianism's goal is human welfare; its optimization procedure ("always maximize") produces the demandingness problem — you can never rest while others suffer.
- The text explicitly notes the gap between a "clear decision procedure" and morally troubling implications.

**What goes wrong without it:** Defenders point to the appealing target; critics point to the absurd procedure; neither notices that the two are logically distinct and could in principle be decoupled. The dispute becomes a shouting match about whether the goal is nice or the procedure is harsh, with no diagnosis of *which* is doing the work.

---

## 6. Identifying the selection principle in dilemmas

**Definition:** In a case presenting multiple defensible options, asking what mechanism within the framework actually picks one — making the operative criterion explicit.

**Examples from the text:**
- The trolley problem forces the question: what does utilitarianism select — the option that minimizes total harm, or the option that respects rights? The framework must nominate a tie-breaker.
- The drowning child forces the question: what selects whether a distant child is one's moral concern — proximity, cost, or number? Utilitarianism must specify.

**What goes wrong without it:** Frameworks appear to handle dilemmas when in fact they merely restate them in new vocabulary. Without identifying the selection principle, a framework can be accused of failing precisely where it claimed clarity.

---

## 7. Deriving logical entailments against intuitions

**Definition:** Stating what a principle *logically commits one to* and checking whether that commitment is one a reasonable person can accept — using the entailment itself as the objection.

**Examples from the text:**
- Singer derives that utilitarianism commits us to near-total sacrifice of income and comfort; the entailment, not the abstract principle, is the objection.
- Nozick derives that pure hedonism commits us to preferring wired-in illusion; the entailment is the objection.

**What goes wrong without it:** A principle can be intellectually admired while its consequences remain hidden. Without deriving entailments, defenders can treat the principle as a clean abstraction disconnected from what it actually demands.

---

## 8. Distinguishing procedural clarity from substantive adequacy

**Definition:** Separating whether a framework *yields a determinate answer* from whether that answer *tracks moral truth* — refusing to conflate "decidable" with "right."

**Examples from the text:**
- The conclusion explicitly notes that utilitarianism provides a "clear decision procedure" yet its implications "conflict with our moral intuitions about rights, justice, and personal integrity."
- The whole arc of the passage moves from procedural clarity (definition, application) to substantive inadequacy (the objections).

**What goes wrong without it:** Decidability gets mistaken for vindication. A framework with no answers at all is easily criticized; one that *always* answers is easily mistaken for one that answers *well*. Without this distinction, formal elegance substitutes for moral correctness.

---

## 9. Reconstructing a framework more charitably than presented

**Definition:** Before attacking a view, modifying it in the strongest plausible direction so that criticism engages the best version, not the straw version.

**Examples from the text:**
- Mill "tries to address" the justice problem not by abandoning utilitarianism but by enriching it with qualitative rankings — a charitable move that strengthens the theory before the next objection.
- Singer similarly assumes that utilitarians should take their own maximization seriously, refusing the easy escape of restricted scope.

**What goes wrong without it:** Critics attack a version of the view the defender never held, producing easy refutations no one accepts. The result is that the strongest version of every view survives unrefuted while only caricatures fall.
