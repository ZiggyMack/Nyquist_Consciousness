## 1. Relocating a problem instead of solving it

**Definition:** When a proposed solution is offered to a difficulty, evaluating whether the solution has actually dissolved the difficulty or merely transferred the same logical burden to a different location in the chain.

**Examples from the text:**
- The objection that "voters can delegate to trusted experts" is met not by rejecting it but by showing the burden reappears: "how does a non-expert evaluate which expert to trust?" The expertise deficit has migrated rather than vanished.
- The cognitive-diversity defense of democracy does not solve the ignorance problem; it relocates the question of whether that diversity is *actually retained* by the aggregation mechanism.
- The reframing from voter cognition to "structural integrity of the information environment" relocates the source of democratic failure from one site to another rather than declaring it absent.

**What goes wrong when absent:** The proposed solution is accepted at face value. The regress of justification is left hidden, and the same difficulty surfaces later under a different name, looking like a new problem rather than the unresolved original.

## 2. Surfacing unstated bridge premises

**Definition:** Identifying an implicit assumption that connects a stated premise to a stated conclusion — the logical glue that would have to hold for the inference to be valid, but that the arguer never made explicit.

**Examples from the text:**
- The conclusion that democratic legitimacy faces "a structural tension with technocratic governance" rests on an unstated principle: that "meaningful" tracking requires expertise, not merely sincere intent. The text never makes this explicit but uses it to bridge Premise 2 to Premise 3.
- Landemore's diversity argument "assumes the aggregation mechanism (voting) preserves the information that diversity generates" — this preservation claim is the unstated bridge between group-level diversity and group-level competence.
- The eventual reframing ("not on the epistemic capacity of individual voters but on the structural integrity of the information environment") rests on an unstated premise that structural properties are the right explanatory level for institutional quality.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Arguments appear self-evident that are in fact parasitic on hidden commitments. Refutation becomes impossible because the disagreement hides inside the invisible glue rather than in the stated claims.

## 3. Conditionalizing a framework on background preconditions

**Definition:** Specifying the worldly or institutional conditions that must hold for an argument's conclusion to be sound, and identifying whether those conditions are in fact satisfied. Separating what the framework *claims* from whether the conditions for the claim to succeed are *met*.

**Examples from the text:**
- "If the aggregation mechanism is vulnerable to manipulation (gerrymandering, media capture, algorithmic amplification), the diversity argument fails." The diversity argument is not attacked on its own terms — its preconditions for success are interrogated.
- The delegation-to-experts solution is conditionalized on voters being able to evaluate trustworthiness, a precondition the text denies.
- The whole tension between democracy and technocracy is conditionalized on whether technical expertise *cannot* be rendered votable (e.g., through translation, simplification, structured deliberation) — a background assumption rather than a logical necessity.

**What goes wrong when absent:** A framework is either endorsed wholesale or dismissed wholesale, with no purchase for distinguishing the conditions under which it would be correct from those under which it would fail. The framework becomes unfalsifiable, or its failures become invisible.

## 4. Shifting the explanatory locus

**Definition:** Moving the level at which a phenomenon is explained — from individual-level to structural/institutional-level, from proximate to distal, from agent to system. Re-routing the causal or constitutive account to a different scale of analysis.

**Examples from the text:**
- "The quality of democratic institutions depends **not on the epistemic capacity of individual voters but on** the structural integrity of the information environment in which they operate." This is a paradigmatic locus shift: from micro (individual cognition) to macro (information ecology).
- The delegation objection shifts the locus from voter competence to expert-trustworthiness competence — and then is shown to be inadequate at the new locus.
- The vulnerability list (gerrymandering, media capture, algorithmic amplification) reframes democratic quality as a property of the *channel* rather than the *sender or receiver*.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Explanations oscillate between levels without committing. Individual-level interventions (civics education, voter information) are proposed for structural-level problems, or vice versa. The diagnosis is mismatched to the actual mechanism.
