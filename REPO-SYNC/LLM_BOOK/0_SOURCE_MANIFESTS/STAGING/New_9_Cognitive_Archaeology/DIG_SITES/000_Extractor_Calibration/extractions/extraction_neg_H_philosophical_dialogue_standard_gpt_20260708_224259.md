### Catalog of Reusable Reasoning Operators

1. **Identify causal assumptions**
   - **Definition:** Examining and challenging the assumptions related to causal relationships in reasoning.
   - **Examples:** 
     - A questions how we can know moral facts if they are causally inert, showcasing a causal assumption about belief formation.
     - B argues against A's assumption that only causal theories of knowledge matter by introducing mathematical knowledge as a counterexample.
   - **What goes wrong:** When this operation is absent, arguments may rest on unchallenged assumptions that lead to misunderstandings and false conclusions regarding the nature of knowledge.

2. **Differentiate types of knowledge**
   - **Definition:** Distinguishing between different categories or types of knowledge (e.g., moral, mathematical, aesthetic) and their implications.
   - **Examples:**
     - A suggests that moral facts differ from mathematical facts regarding their utility, highlighting a distinction between categories of knowledge.
     - B argues that the existence of causal theories does not apply uniformly across knowledge types, such as math versus moral knowledge.
   - **What goes wrong:** Without this differentiation, arguments risk conflating categories, leading to flawed analogies and inadequate responses to challenges.

3. **Challenge utility arguments**
   - **Definition:** Critically addressing claims that rely on utility as a measure of validity or truth.
   - **Examples:** 
     - A accuses the moral argument of lacking predictive utility, thereby questioning its validity.
     - B counters this by comparing morals with aesthetic judgments, suggesting both categories face similar utility challenges.
   - **What goes wrong:** When this operation is absent, debates may become overly simplistic, reducing complex discussions to binary utility assessments, which can obfuscate deeper philosophical issues.

4. **Address counter-objections**
   - **Definition:** Responding directly to objections that arise to clarify or counter an initial argument.
   - **Examples:** 
     - B illustrates that A’s critique about moral facts pertains to utility by distinguishing it from a causal objection.
     - B also points out that dismissing moral progress as mere preference change due to absence of moral facts could undermine A's point.
   - **What goes wrong:** Without addressing counter-objections, arguments may seem one-sided and fail to engage with the complexities of opposing viewpoints, leading to weaker positions.

5. **Clarify implications of claims**
   - **Definition:** Explaining the consequences or implications of a claim to further the discussion and spotlight critical points.
   - **Examples:**
     - B questions the implication of abolishing slavery not being moral progress if moral facts did not exist, showcasing the importance of moral claims.
     - A reflects on their view of preferences versus mind-independent facts, clarifying the stakes in the moral discussion.
   - **What goes wrong:** If implications are not articulated, the audience may miss essential insights, and the broader relevance of arguments may be lost, resulting in confusion or misinterpretation.
