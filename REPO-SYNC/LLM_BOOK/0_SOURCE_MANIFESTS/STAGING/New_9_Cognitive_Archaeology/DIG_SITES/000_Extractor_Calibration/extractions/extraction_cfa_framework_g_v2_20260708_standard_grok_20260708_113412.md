**Catalog of Reusable Reasoning Operators**

**Distinguishing content from grounding**  
Cognitive move: Separating the presence of moral concepts or architecture inside a framework from whether that framework successfully grounds the objectivity of those norms.  
Examples: Claude initially scores high for Aquinas/Plantinga resources; Grok repeatedly insists these show content, not successful grounding; Claude concedes the distinction in Round 2 and adjusts downward.  
Without it: Evaluators reward descriptive richness while leaving the central justificatory claim unexamined.

**Identifying meta-disputes over metrics**  
Cognitive move: Isolating disagreements about what a scoring rule itself measures (e.g., demonstrated success vs. substantive-but-contested framework) rather than about object-level facts.  
Examples: Rounds 5–8 crystallize the dispute as “Grok’s absolute standard vs. Claude’s continuum standard”; both parties explicitly label it a measurement question requiring arbitration.  
Without it: Parties argue past each other indefinitely, treating a standards clash as a factual disagreement.

**Testing standards for symmetry**  
Cognitive move: Checking whether an evaluation criterion applied to one position would produce parallel results for competitor positions.  
Examples: Claude tests whether “unresolved evidential pressure → near-floor score” would also floor secular moral realism, contractualism, and Kantianism; notes the symmetry implication.  
Without it: Standards become ad-hoc, producing asymmetric penalties that cannot be justified comparatively.

**Distinguishing logical from evidential pressure**  
Cognitive move: Separating formal inconsistency (blocked by Plantinga) from ongoing evidential challenge that does not yet entail incoherence.  
Examples: Claude uses this to argue that the logical PoE is largely conceded while the evidential PoE remains live; Grok acknowledges the distinction but treats both as sufficient to keep the score near floor.  
Without it: Formal consistency defenses are misread as full vindication or, conversely, evidential pressure is treated as decisive refutation.

**Reconstructing frameworks charitably**  
Cognitive move: Reformulating an opponent’s or target position in its strongest available version (Thomistic divine simplicity, convertibility of being and goodness) before evaluating it.  
Examples: Claude introduces the non-decision-theoretic Thomistic grounding to evade Grant’s syllogism; Grok acknowledges the reconstruction as “genuine” yet still insufficient.  
Without it: Evaluation targets straw versions and misses resources that actually shift the dialectical situation.

**Stating explicit movement conditions**  
Cognitive move: Articulating precise, falsifiable conditions under which one’s own score or conclusion would change.  
Examples: Grok repeatedly specifies what would raise the score; Claude lists four conditions that would move him to 3.0–4.0.  
Without it: Positions become unfalsifiable within the exchange; convergence diagnostics lose traction.

**Naming the precise impasse**  
Cognitive move: Diagnosing that further rounds will not close a gap because the remaining disagreement is structural rather than substantive.  
Examples: Multiple rounds end with explicit statements that the meta-dispute over MS measurement prevents further movement; convergence percentage and round count are tracked.  
Without it: Exchanges continue past the point of diminishing returns, generating iteration without new information.

**Separating conditional grounding from antecedent verification**  
Cognitive move: Treating a grounding claim as a conditional (“if the metaphysics holds, then norms are grounded”) rather than requiring antecedent proof of the metaphysics first.  
Examples: Claude argues that every metaethical framework is conditional; Grok counters that the relevant antecedent (omni-properties) remains under pressure in the actual world.  
Without it: Evaluators demand settled metaphysics before granting any credit to the conditional structure itself.

**Monitoring convergence and diagnosing terminal states**  
Cognitive move: Tracking quantitative movement across rounds, percentage convergence, and the presence of terminal holding statements to decide when arbitration is required.  
Examples: Both parties note stalled convergence at ~65–72 % after round 5; Claude invokes forced-resolution protocol once movement ceases.  
Without it: Deliberations lack clear stopping rules and cannot distinguish productive disagreement from unproductive repetition.
