# Catalog of Reasoning Operators

---

## 1. Forcing Parallel Case Comparison

**Cognitive move:** When an opponent raises a structural objection against X, identify a domain Y that shares the same structure but which the opponent already accepts, forcing them to either extend their objection consistently or abandon it.

**Examples from text:**
- "Mathematical facts are also causally inert, yet we have mathematical knowledge" — B uses math to neutralize A's causal inertness objection
- "Aesthetic facts don't predict bridge loads either, but you presumably think some paintings are better than others" — B uses aesthetics to neutralize A's utility objection

**What goes wrong when absent:** Arguments succeed by local plausibility rather than structural validity. Objections that would destroy accepted domains if applied consistently are never tested for scope, allowing motivated asymmetric treatment of similar cases.

---

## 2. Tracking Objection Substitution Mid-Argument

**Cognitive move:** Detecting when an opponent quietly replaces one objection with a structurally different one, naming the shift explicitly to prevent the rhetorical accumulation of distinct arguments as though they were a single reinforcing case.

**Examples from text:**
- "That's a different objection — now you're arguing from utility, not causation" — B catches A sliding from a causal-pathway argument to an empirical-utility argument
- Implicitly, B tracks that A's third move (preference-expression) is yet a third distinct position

**What goes wrong when absent:** The opponent appears to be delivering converging evidence when they are actually cycling through independent and potentially inconsistent objections, each abandoned when challenged but collectively creating a false impression of overwhelming force.

---

## 3. Identifying What a Position Must Explain

**Cognitive move:** Rather than directly attacking a claim, specifying the *explanandum* that any adequate version of the opponent's position must account for, shifting burden of proof to phenomena the opponent has not addressed.

**Examples from text:**
- "Then explain why moral progress is intelligible" — B demands A's preference-only view account for the directionality of moral change
- "The phenomenology of moral reasoning — the sense that we're discovering something rather than inventing it — is evidence that needs explaining" — B identifies a datum that deflationary views must still handle

**What goes wrong when absent:** Positions are evaluated only on what they positively assert, never on what they must but cannot explain. Reductive accounts escape scrutiny by remaining silent on recalcitrant data.

---

## 4. Distinguishing Structural Objection from Decisive Refutation

**Cognitive move:** Acknowledging that an objection identifies a genuine challenge while denying it constitutes a knock-down argument, because parallel cases show that structural challenges of this type are survivable.

**Examples from text:**
- "The platonist about math faces the same challenge and has responses — rational intuition, indispensability arguments, structural correspondence" — B shows the causal inertness problem is a known, contested challenge, not a closed case
- "Even if the explanation turns out to be deflationary" — B concedes the phenomenological evidence may ultimately be explained away, but insists it must be explained

**What goes wrong when absent:** Structural difficulties are treated as automatic refutations. The mere existence of a hard question is conflated with the nonexistence of an answer, producing false confidence in eliminative conclusions.

---

## 5. Separating Descriptive Report from Ontological Commitment

**Cognitive move:** Distinguishing between what a speaker *says* or *experiences* and what *facts must exist* for that saying or experience to be what they claim it is, exposing hidden ontological commitments in apparently modest first-person reports.

**Examples from text:**
- "I think I'm expressing preferences, not tracking mind-independent facts" — A attempts to retreat to a purely descriptive self-report
- B's response forces A to confront that this self-description conflicts with A's own apparent belief in moral progress as progress rather than mere change

**What goes wrong when absent:** Speakers successfully retreat to seemingly modest phenomenological or linguistic reports while avoiding scrutiny of the metaphysical commitments their behavior and further beliefs implicitly require.

---

## 6. Appealing to Behavioral Belief Over Stated Belief

**Cognitive move:** When an opponent's stated position conflicts with their demonstrated or presumed actual behavior and judgments, using that behavioral evidence to show the stated position is not genuinely held, creating internal pressure on the position.

**Examples from text:**
- "But you don't actually believe that" — B argues A cannot sincerely hold that abolishing slavery is mere preference-change given how A reasons about history
- The implicit challenge: A's willingness to call abolition *progress* reveals commitment to something beyond preference-reporting

**What goes wrong when absent:** Stated philosophical positions are evaluated in isolation from the first-person commitments speakers actually operate with, allowing positions to survive by never being tested against the speaker's own practice.

---

## 7. Proving Too Much as Reductio

**Cognitive move:** Accepting an opponent's principle temporarily and demonstrating that it eliminates not just the target conclusion but also conclusions the opponent is committed to preserving, revealing the principle is too broad to be accepted.

**Examples from text:**
- "It proves too much: aesthetic facts don't predict bridge loads either" — B shows A's utility criterion, if applied consistently, eliminates aesthetic discourse A presumably endorses
- Implicit in the math parallel: A's causal criterion, if maintained, also eliminates mathematical knowledge

**What goes wrong when absent:** Eliminative arguments succeed locally without being tested for whether their operative principle, if universalized, destroys the opponent's own commitments elsewhere.

---

## 8. Treating Phenomenology as Evidence Requiring Explanation

**Cognitive move:** Elevating first-person experiential character — not as proof of the reality of its apparent object, but as a datum that any adequate theory must account for, even a theory that ultimately deflates it.

**Examples from text:**
- "The phenomenology of moral reasoning — the sense that we're discovering something rather than inventing it — is evidence that needs explaining" — B does not claim this settles the debate, only that the experience is a theoretical constraint
- The framing "even if the explanation turns out to be deflationary" shows B is not naively reifying the phenomenology

**What goes wrong when absent:** Deflationary or eliminative theories are permitted to simply ignore the character of first-person experience rather than being required to explain why that experience presents as it does. Theories face no constraint from the phenomena they purport to explain.
