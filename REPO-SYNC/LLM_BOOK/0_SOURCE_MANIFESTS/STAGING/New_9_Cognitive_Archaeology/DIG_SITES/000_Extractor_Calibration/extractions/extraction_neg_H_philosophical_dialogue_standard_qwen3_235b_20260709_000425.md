### 1. **Challenging a hidden assumption**

**Definition**: Identifying and questioning an unstated presupposition that underlies an argument, especially one that limits the space of possible responses.

**Examples**:
- B challenges A’s assumption that *only causally efficacious facts can be known*, pointing out that this rules out mathematical knowledge unless alternative epistemologies are considered.
- B implicitly challenges A’s assumption that *epistemic access requires causal interaction*, by invoking mathematical platonism as a counterexample.

**What goes wrong without it**: Arguments proceed on unexamined foundations, leading to false dilemmas or premature dismissals of entire domains (e.g., dismissing moral knowledge simply because it lacks causal powers).

### 2. **Drawing analogies across domains**

**Definition**: Invoking structurally similar cases from a different domain to test or challenge a principle, often to expose inconsistency or overgeneralization.

**Examples**:
- B draws an analogy between *moral facts* and *mathematical facts*, both of which are causally inert, to challenge A’s claim that causal inertness precludes knowledge.
- B later compares moral facts to *aesthetic facts*, arguing that if we accept evaluative judgments in art without predictive utility, we should not reject moral facts solely on that basis.

**What goes wrong without it**: Reasoning becomes domain-locked and fails to detect when principles are being applied selectively or inconsistently.

### 3. **Distinguishing map from territory**

**Definition**: Separating our representations, beliefs, or linguistic practices from the reality they purport to describe, especially to avoid conflating epistemology with ontology.

**Examples**:
- B distinguishes between *the phenomenology of moral discovery* (how moral reasoning feels) and *the existence of moral facts* (whether there’s anything real being discovered), using the former as evidence for the latter while acknowledging the need for explanation.
- A treats *expressing preferences* as incompatible with *tracking mind-independent facts*, implicitly separating subjective attitudes from objective truth-makers.

**What goes wrong without it**: One may confuse psychological experience (e.g., feeling certain) with ontological commitment, or dismiss realism merely because beliefs are psychologically mediated.

### 4. **Separating ontology from epistemology**

**Definition**: Distinguishing questions about *what exists* from questions about *how we know*, to prevent invalid inferences from epistemic limitations to ontological nonexistence.

**Examples**:
- B resists A’s inference from “we can’t causally interact with moral facts” (epistemological concern) to “moral facts don’t exist” (ontological conclusion), noting that the same applies to mathematics.
- A attempts to tie existence to knowability via causal mechanisms, but B decouples these, arguing that *existence* and *access* are distinct issues.

**What goes wrong without it**: People commit the *epistemic fallacy* — concluding that because we cannot observe or causally interact with X, X does not exist.

### 5. **Identifying category errors**

**Definition**: Recognizing when a concept is being applied outside its proper domain or when properties from one category are incorrectly attributed to another.

**Examples**:
- B points out that A commits a category error by demanding *predictive utility* from moral facts, a criterion appropriate for scientific theories but not necessarily for normative or evaluative domains.
- The remark “you can’t build a bridge with ‘murder is wrong’” treats moral facts like engineering tools — B shows this misclassifies the function of moral claims.

**What goes wrong without it**: Criteria for legitimacy are misapplied across domains, leading to dismissals based on irrelevant standards (e.g., judging ethics by technological output).

### 6. **Reconstructing charitably**

**Definition**: Interpreting an opponent’s argument in its strongest possible form, even if not explicitly stated, to engage with its best version.

**Examples**:
- B interprets A’s objection not merely as “no causal access → no knowledge” but as potentially rooted in a broader epistemological framework (causal theories of knowledge), then offers targeted counterexamples.
- B doesn’t reduce A’s position to “morals aren’t real because they don’t push particles,” but instead explores the underlying epistemic principle and tests its scope.

**What goes wrong without it**: Dialogue devolves into straw-manning; arguments are knocked down in weak forms while their robust versions go unchallenged.

### 7. **Separating descriptive from normative claims**

**Definition**: Distinguishing statements about how things *are* from how they *ought to be*, or distinguishing explanatory accounts from prescriptive ones.

**Examples**:
- B notes that even if moral beliefs arise from psychological processes (descriptive), this doesn’t settle whether those beliefs track objective truths (normative).
- A claims to “express preferences,” which is a descriptive claim about his own psychology, but B presses whether this stance can account for normative phenomena like *moral progress*.

**What goes wrong without it**: Descriptive accounts are taken to exhaust normative ones (e.g., “morality is just emotion” used to imply “nothing is truly right or wrong”).

### 8. **Asking what selects an outcome**

**Definition**: Probing what mechanism or principle determines why one belief, value, or state is selected over another — especially in evolutionary, psychological, or social contexts.

**Examples**:
- B implicitly asks: *What selects for moral progress if not convergence toward moral truths?* If slavery’s abolition is just preference change, what explains its directionality?
- A’s causal argument implies: *Physical causation selects beliefs* — so B asks how non-physical facts could play any role in selection.

**What goes wrong without it**: Change is labeled “progress” without explaining why it isn’t arbitrary; belief formation is described without identifying what guides it toward truth.

### 9. **Identifying an unstated bridge premise**

**Definition**: Detecting a missing but necessary assumption that connects two parts of an argument, often revealing vulnerability in the logic.

**Examples**:
- B identifies the bridge premise in A’s argument: *Only causally efficacious entities can be known*. Without this, the jump from “moral facts don’t push particles” to “we can’t know moral facts” fails.
- A relies on the unstated premise: *Usefulness in empirical prediction is necessary for factual status* — B exposes this by showing it would eliminate aesthetics too.

**What goes wrong without it**: Arguments appear valid on the surface but rest on unexamined, often controversial assumptions.

### 10. **Distinguishing what a framework claims from whether it succeeds**

**Definition**: Holding a theory accountable for its own stated aims, without conflating failure to achieve them with incoherence or irrelevance.

**Examples**:
- B acknowledges that platonists face a challenge in explaining knowledge of abstracta, but insists the *framework’s goal* — explaining objectivity — is still worth pursuing, even if current solutions (e.g., rational intuition) are imperfect.
- B doesn’t deny that moral realism has explanatory burdens, but separates the *project* from its current success, saying the phenomenology of discovery “needs explaining, even if… deflationary.”

**What goes wrong without it**: Theories are dismissed for being incomplete or imperfect rather than fundamentally flawed, shutting down inquiry prematurely.

---

### 11. **Separating measurement dimensions that are conflated**

**Definition**: Disentangling distinct criteria (e.g., causal efficacy, predictive power, explanatory indispensability) that an argument treats as a single standard.

**Examples**:
- A conflates *causal inertness*, *lack of predictive utility*, and *epistemic inaccessibility* as jointly disqualifying moral facts. B separates them: math is causally inert but useful; aesthetics aren’t predictive but still involve evaluation.
- B shows that *utility* and *causation* are different axes: one pertains to application, the other to ontology/epistemology.

**What goes wrong without it**: Multiple distinct issues are bundled into one objection, making rebuttal harder and masking where the real disagreement lies.

---

### 12. **Appealing to empirical track record against logical objection**

**Definition**: Using practical success or historical coherence as evidence for a framework’s legitimacy, even in the face of theoretical or metaphysical objections.

**Examples**:
- A appeals to the *empirical utility of mathematics* (“you can build bridges”) to argue that math earns its ontological keep in a way morality doesn’t.
- B counters not by denying utility’s importance, but by showing that *other non-predictive domains* (aesthetics) still support objective-seeming judgments.

**What goes wrong without it**: Frameworks are judged purely on abstract coherence, ignoring their ability to make sense of real-world practices like scientific modeling or moral reform.
