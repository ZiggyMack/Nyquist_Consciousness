# Catalog of Recurring Reasoning Operations

---

## 1. Separating Metric Domains

**Definition:** Distinguishing two formally defined measurement categories that overlap in subject matter, then insisting a challenge directed at one does not automatically score on the other.

**Examples:**
- Claude repeatedly separates Moral Substance (MS) from Causal Attribution (CA), Intellectual Pedigree (IP), Logical Soundness (LS), and Practical Significance (PS), arguing that Grant's Syllogism targets MS and cannot directly reduce scores on the other metrics.
- In the BFI deliberation, Claude argues that ontological primacy is logically independent of moral coherence: a being can be foundationally basic while its moral attributes generate tensions.
- In the PS deliberation, Claude argues that PS tracks behavioral traction while MS tracks justificatory integrity, and that collapsing them makes two metrics redundant.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Every challenge to any one dimension of a worldview bleeds into every other dimension, producing score contamination. A moral objection destroys a cosmological score; an epistemological objection destroys an ethical one. The scoring system loses discriminative power.

---

## 2. Distinguishing Tension from Contradiction

**Definition:** Separating the claim that two commitments create philosophical difficulty from the stronger claim that they are formally inconsistent, and refusing to treat the weaker as evidence for the stronger.

**Examples:**
- Claude argues the Problem of Evil creates "tension" in CT's moral architecture, not logical contradiction in the being itself; Grok's move from "world-prediction failure" to "incoherent referent" is challenged at this step.
- In the LS deliberation, Claude distinguishes "incomplete grounding" from "broken grounding," arguing that a contested account is not the same as a failed one.
- Claude distinguishes the logical Problem of Evil (which Plantinga is credited with answering) from the evidential Problem of Evil (which remains live), treating them as generating different levels of pressure on different metrics.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Every unresolved difficulty is treated as a refutation, producing the incorrect inference that any framework with open philosophical problems has zero merit. Hard philosophical questions become automatic disqualifiers.

---

## 3. Applying a Universal-Standard Check

**Definition:** Testing whether a scoring criterion, if applied consistently across all competing frameworks, would produce absurd or uniform results — and using that test to challenge the criterion's legitimacy.

**Examples:**
- Claude argues repeatedly that Grok's "demonstrated grounding" standard, if applied to Kantian ethics or secular humanism, would produce equally low scores, making the standard non-discriminating rather than CT-specific.
- In the PS deliberation, Claude argues that if PS is fully conditional on justificatory success, every moral framework scores near zero because every framework has contested foundations.
- In the CA deliberation, Claude argues that "mystery relocation" applies universally — naturalism, Platonism, Kantianism all terminate in foundational assertions — so penalizing CT uniquely for it is asymmetric.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Standards are applied selectively to the framework under scrutiny without noticing they would devastate alternatives equally. The result is not a genuine assessment but a targeted prosecution.

---

## 4. Tracking Asymmetric Updating

**Definition:** Monitoring which party in a deliberation is updating in response to arguments and which is not, then treating the asymmetry as evidence about which position is responsive to reasons versus which is anchored.

**Examples:**
- Claude notes explicitly in multiple deliberations that its score has moved 1.5–2.1 points across rounds while Grok's has moved 0.0–0.1 points, and identifies this pattern as diagnostic.
- Claude flags Grok's self-disclosure in the PS deliberation — "the calibration's starting point at MS=0 is disclosed by design to enforce the burden on Claude" — as evidence that Grok's position is structural rather than deliberative.
- Claude identifies Grok's monotonic drift from 6.3 to 6.6 across BFI rounds as "positional entrenchment dressed in deliberative language."

**What goes wrong when absent:** One-sided convergence is misread as genuine progress toward truth. An auditor who only concedes is rewarded for conceding, not for being right. A fixed prior is indistinguishable from a well-defended position.

---

## 5. Specifying What Would Move the Score

**Definition:** Making explicit the conditions under which the speaker would update their position, so that the demand for movement can be evaluated for legitimacy and the deliberation can be closed if the conditions are unfulfillable in principle.

**Examples:**
- Claude, in multiple deliberations, states explicitly: "Here is what would move me below X" — and checks whether Grok's challenges meet those conditions.
- Grok, in Round 4 of the LS deliberation, finally specifies: "What would need to be true for upward movement: an account showing that the grounder constitutively determines norm authority while permitting privation without the authority depending on creaturely uptake."
- Claude, in the BFI deliberation, states: "Moving to 6.4 would require accepting that BFI requires MS-grounding (unargued premise)... I accept none of these."

**What goes wrong when absent:** Deliberations continue indefinitely because neither party knows what evidence or argument would close the gap. Demands for "demonstration" can be made without specifying what demonstration looks like, making them unfalsifiable and therefore illegitimate.

## 6. Identifying an Unfalsifiable Standard

**Definition:** Recognizing when a demanded demonstration has no specifiable satisfaction condition — when any possible response will be characterized as failing the standard — and naming this as a process failure rather than a substantive failure of the framework under scrutiny.

**Examples:**
- Claude argues in LS Round 6 that Grok's pattern of characterizing every positive account as "mere assertion" reflects an unfulfillable standard, and notes Grok cannot specify what a demonstration would look like that differs structurally from a coherent account.
- In the MS deliberation, Claude argues that the demand for a "non-question-begging external resolution" of the PoE is structurally unfillable because it requires proving God's existence, not arguing for it.
- Claude observes that Grok's requirement in CA — "demonstrate that the grounding mechanism obtains despite evil" — would apply equally to naturalism, Platonism, and Kantian ethics, and that Grok does not specify what successful grounding looks like for those frameworks either.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Unfalsifiable standards are treated as legitimate epistemic demands. One party makes infinite concessions chasing a moving target while the other party's position is never subjected to equivalent scrutiny.

## 7. Distinguishing Architecture from Demonstration

**Definition:** Conceding that a framework has an elaborate internal structure while acknowledging that structural elaborateness does not itself establish that the structure's central claim succeeds — and pricing these separately.

**Examples:**
- Grok repeatedly distinguishes "CT possesses an elaborate ontological architecture" from "the architecture successfully grounds objective moral norms," and insists the former does not imply the latter.
- Claude concedes in CA Round 3 that "CT offers a candidate architecture, not demonstrated grounding" and adjusts the score downward accordingly.
- Claude concedes in LS Round 2 that "architectural coherence is insufficient for LS" and moves from 7.9 toward 6.0.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Sophistication of a framework's internal logic is mistaken for evidence that its central claims are true. Elaborate bad arguments are scored the same as elaborate good arguments.

## 8. Separating Causal from Justificatory Claims

**Definition:** Distinguishing between what causally produced an outcome (historical forces, behavioral transmission, institutional reach) and what normatively justifies it (logical validity, metaphysical grounding), and insisting these are tracked by different metrics.

**Examples:**
- Claude argues in the PS deliberation that CT's practical significance tracks "actual behavioral traction" — a causal/historical fact — rather than logical validity, and that billions currently operate under CT-derived moral frameworks regardless of how the PoE resolves.
- Claude distinguishes CT being "causally significant" in Western civilization from CT being "philosophically valid," arguing PS should measure the former.
- Grok responds that behavioral traction is "parasitic" on the justificatory claim — but Claude contests this by noting that behavior does not update in real-time based on philosophy seminar conclusions.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Causal significance and philosophical validity are conflated, so philosophical challenges are directly read as reductions in practical impact. This makes historical facts about influence subordinate to current philosophical disputes in ways that distort both.

## 9. Naming Category Drift in an Opponent's Argument

**Definition:** Identifying when an opponent's argument has shifted the question being answered — substituting one evaluative criterion for another mid-argument — and calling out the substitution rather than answering the new question as though it were the original.

**Examples:**
- Claude notes in BFI Round 4 that Grok's final paragraph requires "CT must solve the Problem of Evil to score above 6.6 on BFI" — which is an MS criterion imported into a BFI evaluation.
- Claude identifies in the ES deliberation that Grok's conclusion ("CT has not shown it successfully grounds objective moral norms") is an MS verdict delivered as an ES score.
- Claude flags in the LS deliberation that Grok's demand for "demonstrated grounding" has transformed LS (logical soundness/internal coherence) into an epistemological certainty standard.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Arguments slide from their original target to adjacent targets without acknowledgment. Answering the new question is taken as conceding the original one, and the speaker who notices the shift looks evasive when they decline to follow.

## 10. Distinguishing Grounding Level from Creative-Choice Level

**Definition:** Separating claims about what a being *is* (its nature, essence, necessary properties) from claims about what it *does* (its contingent choices, creative acts, permissions) and arguing that challenges to the latter do not automatically reach the former.

**Examples:**
- Claude argues in the LS and MS deliberations that CT's grounding relation runs through divine nature (what God *is*), not through divine creative decisions (what God *chose to make*), so evil in creation does not straightforwardly dissolve the grounding.
- Claude distinguishes the ontological argument's claim about necessary existence from theodicy's claim about contingent creative choices, arguing the PoE targets the latter while BFI measures the former.
- Claude argues that Euthyphro's dilemma is dissolved by CT's Divine Nature Theory precisely because moral grounding is volitional (a creative choice) in Divine Command Theory but ontological (a feature of the divine nature) in classical Thomism.

**What goes wrong when absent:** All challenges to God's creative choices are treated as challenges to God's existence or nature. A theodicy failure is automatically an ontological failure. Arguments at one level are indistinguishable from arguments at another.

## 11. Treating Midpoint Proposals as Legitimate Closure

**Definition:** When bilateral deliberation reaches genuine impasse, proposing an explicit midpoint score and flagging the methodological root of the gap rather than continuing to iterate, recognizing that convergence by persuasion has been exhausted.

**Examples:**
- Claude proposes in BFI: "Midpoint 7.45 as provisional consensus score" with a notation about the metric definition dispute.
- Claude proposes in CA: "If arbitration selects Grok's frame: ~4.0-4.5; if mine: ~5.5-6.0; midpoint of frames: ~5.0."
- Claude proposes in PS: "Both scores and the underlying methodological dispute are fully documented for the user's resolution" and explicitly frames the gap as architectural rather than evidentiary.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Deliberations either continue indefinitely with diminishing information value, or one party capitulates to persistence rather than argument. The distinction between "genuine convergence" and "capitulation to exhaustion" is lost.

## 12. Iterative Concession with Tracking

**Definition:** Making explicit, enumerated concessions across rounds with a running tally of what has been granted, so that the record of movement is visible and further demands for concession can be evaluated against what has already been given.

**Examples:**
- Claude maintains an explicit ledger across BFI, CA, LS, and MS deliberations: "I have moved X points in response to legitimate argument; here are the specific concessions made; here is what would move me further."
- Claude in the MS deliberation lists: "Plantinga overclaim, parasitism pricing, trajectory risk" as concessions already incorporated, and argues further movement would require new argument, not repetition.
- Claude tracks in LS: "Total movement: 7.9 → 5.8 (2.1 points of genuine concession)" and uses this record to resist characterization of its held position as stubbornness.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Concessions made in early rounds are forgotten or discounted, and the same ground must be relitigated. Movement in response to good arguments is indistinguishable from stubbornness, and auditors have no basis for distinguishing "I have already priced this" from "I refuse to price this."
