Here is a catalog of reasoning operations identified in the text.

### 1. Identifying an Unstated Premise
*   **Definition:** The cognitive move of pinpointing and questioning an implicit assumption that is necessary for an argument to work, but which has not been explicitly stated by the speaker.
*   **Examples from the text:**
    1.  A's argument relies on the idea that knowledge must come from causal interaction.
    2.  B explicitly identifies and challenges this: "You're assuming causal theories of knowledge are the only game in town."
*   **What goes wrong without it:** One might accept a flawed argument because its explicit steps are logical, failing to see that the entire structure rests on a questionable or false foundation that was never brought to light. The core of the disagreement remains unaddressed.

### 2. Testing a Principle with Analogy
*   **Definition:** Applying an opponent's stated principle or argumentative structure to a different, analogous domain to see if it produces an absurd, inconsistent, or undesired conclusion.
*   **Examples from the text:**
    1.  B applies A's "no causal access, no knowledge" principle to mathematics: "Mathematical facts are also causally inert, yet we have mathematical knowledge."
    2.  B applies A's new "must have empirical utility" principle to aesthetics: "aesthetic facts don't predict bridge loads either, but you presumably think some paintings are better than others."
*   **What goes wrong without it:** A flawed or overly narrow principle is accepted because it seems plausible in the single context being discussed. The principle's broader, problematic implications are never discovered.

### 3. Distinguishing Argumentative Criteria
*   **Definition:** The move of noticing and explicitly flagging when an opponent shifts the standard of evaluation or the core principle of their argument midway through the discussion.
*   **Examples from the text:**
    1.  A starts with an argument from causal inertness. When challenged, A shifts to an argument from empirical utility.
    2.  B identifies this shift directly: "That's a different objection — now you're arguing from utility, not causation."
*   **What goes wrong without it:** The "goalposts are moved" without anyone noticing. The conversation becomes confused and unproductive as participants argue past each other, defending against objections that are no longer the ones on the table.

### 4. Separating Ontology from Psychology
*   **Definition:** The move of distinguishing claims about what exists independently in the world (ontology) from claims about our internal, subjective experience (psychology). This is often used to re-categorize a domain as being about preference rather than fact.
*   **Examples from the text:**
    1.  To escape B's analogy about aesthetics, A recasts the entire domain from one of fact to one of preference: "I think I'm expressing preferences, not tracking mind-independent facts."
    2.  B brings this distinction up again by pointing out the "phenomenology of moral reasoning," separating the *feeling* of discovery from the claim of actual discovery.
*   **What goes wrong without it:** Subjective feelings are mistaken for objective truths, or vice-versa. People assume that because something *feels* true or important, it must correspond to a mind-independent reality, or conversely, that a lack of objective proof means the subjective experience is meaningless.

### 5. Testing a Framework's Implications
*   **Definition:** Taking an opponent's proposed framework or explanation and applying it to a concrete, high-stakes example to see if the resulting conclusion is plausible or one the opponent is actually willing to accept.
*   **Examples from the text:**
    1.  B takes A's newly proposed framework ("morality is just preference") and applies it to a settled case of moral improvement.
    2.  B articulates the consequence: "If there are no moral facts, abolishing slavery isn't 'progress' — it's just a preference change. But you don't actually believe that."
*   **What goes wrong without it:** An abstract theory is adopted because it sounds elegant or solves a particular philosophical problem, without anyone checking if it makes a hash of common sense or leads to morally or practically repugnant conclusions in the real world.

### 6. Demanding Explanation for Experience
*   **Definition:** The move of treating subjective experience (phenomenology) as a piece of data that any complete theory must account for, even if the ultimate explanation is that the experience is misleading or illusory.
*   **Examples from the text:**
    1.  B argues that the common feeling we get during moral deliberation is itself a fact that needs to be explained by any moral theory.
    2.  B states: "The phenomenology of moral reasoning — the sense that we're discovering something rather than inventing it — is evidence that needs explaining, even if the explanation turns out to be deflationary."
*   **What goes wrong without it:** A theory can be seen as successful simply by being internally consistent, while completely failing to account for why the world appears to us the way it does. Lived experience is dismissed as "unscientific" or "irrelevant," leaving a crucial explanatory gap.
