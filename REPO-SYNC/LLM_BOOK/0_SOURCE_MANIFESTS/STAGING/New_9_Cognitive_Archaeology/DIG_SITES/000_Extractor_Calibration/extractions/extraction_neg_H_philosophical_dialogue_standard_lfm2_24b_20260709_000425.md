### Catalog of Recurring Reasoning Operators

---

**1. Naming Ontological Commitment**  
- **Definition:** Explicitly identifying and categorizing the assumed entities or structures inherent in a claim.  
- **Examples:**  
  - "Moral facts exist" → commitment to non-physical/law-like entities.  
  - "Mathematical facts are causally inert" → assumption about the metaphysical status of abstractions.  
- **Problem if Absent:** Risk mischaracterizing or misrepresenting the underlying framework or metaphysics of a position.  

---

**2. Challenging the Necessity Condition**  
- **Definition:** Questioning whether a specific condition is truly required for a type of knowledge or property to obtain.  
- **Examples:**  
  - "Causal theories are the only game in town" → demanding one must adopt additionality of causation.  
  - "Murder is wrong doesn't predict bridge loads" → probing whether causation is necessary.  
- **Problem if Absent:** Risk accepting insufficient or conflicted criteria for claiming knowledge/types of facts.

---

**3. Reductive Exemplification**  
- **Definition:** Replacing a more abstract or general claim with one by drawing analogies to more intuitive or concrete cases.  
- **Examples:**  
  - "Building a bridge" as a concretization of utility/benefit.  
  - "Paintings → moral progress" as a reductio on equivalence between categories.  
- **Problem if Absent:** Danger of moving to ungrounded, untestable extremes or concretizations.

---

**4. Questioning Implicit Presuppositions**  
- **Definition:** Identifying and demanding clarification for premises that seem "obvious" or tacit.  
- **Examples:**  
  - "You're assuming causal theories are the only game in town" → exposing unchecked foundational presupposition.  
  - "If moral progress is intelligible... you don't actually believe" → probing what actually counts as progress vs. preference.  
- **Problem if Absent:** Acceptance of dodgy or unexamined premises, undermining logical integrity.

---

**5. Highlighting Entailment/Incommensurability**  
- **Definition:** Warning about unwarranted deductions or comparing or separating types of claims/fact claims vs. normative judgments.  
- **Examples:**  
  - "From utility you're not building bridges" → demarcating descriptive from normative uses.  
  - "Aesthetic success" as analogous to moral progress → highlighting conflation.  
- **Problem if Absent:** Later misapplication by overlapping categories or muddled types of statements (e.g., semantics is misread as geography).

---

**6. Addressing Induction and Explanatory Gaps**  
- **Definition:** Explaining intentionality, structure of beliefs, or progress via mechanisms other than direct causation, focusing on evidence for inference.  
- **Examples:**  
  - "Phenomenology of moral reasoning… needs explaining" → reference to intuitive epistemic route.  
  - "Rational intuition, indispensability arguments…" as explanatory resources for math-knowledge.  
- **Problem if Absent:** Stagnant explanation to non-naturalist or non-empirical dimensions.

---

**7. Exposing Disguised Loaded Questions**  
- **Definition:** Recognizing and challenging arguments that sneak in evaluative moves under descriptive structures.  
- **Examples:**  
  - "You don't actually believe…" → circular challenge about moral progress.
- **Problem if Absent:** Allowing rhetorical moves to substitute genuine interrogatives and harder real-world testing.
