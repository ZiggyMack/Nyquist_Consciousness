1. **Evaluating Outcome Focus**
   - **Definition**: Assessing a moral theory based on its emphasis on the results or consequences of actions rather than preconceived rules or principles.
   - **Examples**: 
     1. Utilitarianism's appeal due to its focus on outcomes.
     2. The contrast with theories that rely on abstract rules instead of consequences.
   - **What Goes Wrong**: When not performed, moral reasoning may overlook the consequences of actions, focusing instead on deontological principles or duties that may not result in the greatest good.

2. **Identifying Hidden Assumptions**
   - **Definition**: Recognizing underlying, implicit premises that are not explicitly stated but affect the argument's validity and persuasiveness.
   - **Examples**: 
     1. Utilitarianism assuming that the greatest good can be quantified and objectively determined.
     2. Mill's assumption that higher pleasures are inherently better or superior without universal agreement on this hierarchy.
   - **What Goes Wrong**: Ignoring hidden assumptions can lead to accepting flawed arguments that seem robust only because their foundational assumptions are not scrutinized.

3. **Addressing Justice Objections**
   - **Definition**: Engaging with criticisms related to fairness and justice within a philosophical framework.
   - **Examples**: 
     1. The problem of justice where utilitarianism could justify actions that harm individuals for the benefit of many.
     2. Nozick's response with the Experience Machine thought experiment to highlight limits of utilitarian calculations.
   - **What Goes Wrong**: Failing to address justice objections can result in ethical theories permitting or endorsing actions that violate strong moral intuitions about fairness and equality.

4. **Challenging With Thought Experiments**
   - **Definition**: Using hypothetical scenarios to test theories by exploring their implications in extreme or unusual conditions.
   - **Examples**: 
     1. The trolley problem challenging the utilitarian justification for harming one to save many.
     2. The Experience Machine suggesting that utility isn't solely based on subjective experience.
   - **What Goes Wrong**: Without thought experiments, theoretical frameworks might not be adequately stress-tested, allowing unnoticed contradictions or unrealistic assumptions to persist.

5. **Highlighting Demandingness Problem**
   - **Definition**: Pointing out the practical burden and potentially unrealistic demands on agents by a moral framework.
   - **Examples**: 
     1. The implication that one can never rest while others are suffering.
     2. Singer’s drowning child scenario to make the demandingness of utilitarian obligations vivid.
   - **What Goes Wrong**: Ignoring the demandingness problem can lead to moral theories demanding too much from individuals, reducing their applicability and acceptance in real-world situations.
