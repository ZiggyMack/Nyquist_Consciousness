# Catalog of Reasoning Operators

---

## 1. Relocating Rather Than Solving a Problem

**Definition:** Showing that a proposed solution doesn't eliminate a difficulty but merely moves it one level up, where the same difficulty reappears in a new form.

**Examples from text:**
- "Voters can delegate to trusted experts" → objection: "how does a non-expert evaluate which expert to trust?" The problem of non-expert evaluation survives the delegation move.
- The diversity-in-aggregation argument relocates the epistemic burden from individual voters to the aggregation mechanism itself.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Apparent solutions are accepted as actual solutions. Patches that shift problems rather than resolve them get mistaken for fixes.

---

## 2. Distinguishing Condition from Mechanism

**Definition:** Separating a necessary input or resource (the condition) from the process that converts it into an output (the mechanism), showing that the presence of the condition does not guarantee the output if the mechanism is broken.

**Examples from text:**
- Cognitive diversity (condition) does not automatically produce good epistemic outcomes unless the aggregation mechanism (voting) faithfully preserves and converts that diversity.
- Citizens may possess evaluative capacity (condition) but still fail to track policy quality if the information environment (mechanism) is corrupted.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Arguments that establish the existence of a resource are treated as establishing that the resource will be used effectively. Presence is conflated with function.

---

## 3. Identifying the Load-Bearing Premise

**Definition:** Isolating the single premise in an argument on which the entire conclusion depends, then targeting that premise specifically rather than the conclusion or surrounding claims.

**Examples from text:**
- The epistemic-democracy argument is granted its premise (cognitive diversity exists) but the load-bearing assumption — that aggregation preserves diversity's information — is isolated and attacked.
- The structural deduction (P1 + P2 + P3 → tension) is accepted, but P3's hidden assumption that "meaningful tracking" requires individual expertise is surfaced and challenged via the delegation counter.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Arguments are attacked at their strongest or most peripheral points rather than their actual point of vulnerability. Rebuttals feel relevant but miss the crux.

---

## 4. Shifting the Unit of Analysis

**Definition:** Moving the explanatory or normative focus from individuals to the structure or system in which individuals are embedded, showing that system-level properties do the real explanatory work.

**Examples from text:**
- Conclusion explicitly shifts from "epistemic capacity of individual voters" to "structural integrity of the information environment."
- The failure mode of democracy is relocated from voter ignorance (individual level) to manipulation of aggregation mechanisms (system level): gerrymandering, media capture, algorithmic amplification.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Individual-level interventions (educating voters, finding better experts) are pursued while structural vulnerabilities remain unaddressed. The diagnosis is systematically at the wrong level.

---

## 5. Separating Descriptive from Normative Adequacy

**Definition:** Distinguishing whether a framework accurately describes a phenomenon from whether the phenomenon, accurately described, meets the normative standard the framework is supposed to vindicate.

**Examples from text:**
- Epistemic democracy may correctly describe what diverse groups *can* do in ideal conditions, but the normative claim (democratic legitimacy is thereby secured) fails if real aggregation mechanisms fall short of ideal conditions.
- Democratic legitimacy is the normative standard; whether actual voting behavior "meaningfully tracks policy quality" is the descriptive question — the text keeps these separate throughout.

**What goes wrong when absent:** A framework is accepted as normatively satisfying because it is descriptively accurate under idealized conditions, ignoring the gap between the model and the actual institution.

---

## 6. Blocking Infinite Regress

**Definition:** Recognizing that an argument generates a regress — where solving problem X requires solving X at a higher level — and naming this explicitly as a structural feature of the problem rather than a soluble sub-problem.

**Examples from text:**
- Non-experts cannot evaluate policies → delegate to experts → but non-experts cannot evaluate which expert to trust → the regress is named ("merely relocates the problem") rather than passed over.
- Implicitly: any further delegation solution (trust institutions to certify experts) would face the same non-expert evaluation problem one level higher.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Each layer of the regress is addressed as if it were the final layer, producing an endless series of patches. The structural character of the problem is never recognized.

---

## 7. Distinguishing Vulnerability from Failure

**Definition:** Separating the claim that a mechanism *can* be compromised under certain conditions from the stronger claim that it *has* or *always does* fail, preserving analytic precision about what has actually been established.

**Examples from text:**
- "If the aggregation mechanism is *vulnerable* to manipulation… the diversity argument fails" — the conditional is maintained; failure is not asserted categorically.
- The structural tension is described as something democracy "faces," not something that has necessarily destroyed democratic legitimacy already.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Identifying a vulnerability is treated as proving actual failure, leading to overclaiming. Alternatively, because actual failure hasn't been proven, the vulnerability is dismissed entirely.
