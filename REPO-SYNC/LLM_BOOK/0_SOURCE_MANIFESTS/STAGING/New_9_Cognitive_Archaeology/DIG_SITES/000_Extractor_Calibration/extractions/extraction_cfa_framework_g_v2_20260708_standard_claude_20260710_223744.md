# Catalog of Reasoning Operators

---

## 1. Separating content presence from mechanism success

**Definition:** Distinguishing between whether a framework *contains* relevant material and whether the *operative mechanism* within that framework actually works as claimed.

**Examples:**
- Claude concedes that CT has rich moral architecture (Aquinas, Augustine, Plantinga) while Grok insists this does not establish that the grounding *relation* succeeds — the presence of moral philosophy is separated from whether the grounding mechanism functions.
- Claude explicitly names this in Round 2: "I conflated these in my initial scoring. Grok is correct that CT can contain sophisticated moral philosophy while the grounding relation remains unestablished."
- Grok repeatedly distinguishes "CT demonstrably has moral content" from "CT's grounding claim is demonstrated to hold," refusing to let content richness substitute for mechanism verification.

**What goes wrong without it:** A framework gets credited for having elaborate internal structure when the operative claim — that the structure *does* what it claims — has never been examined. Evaluation becomes a catalog of features rather than an assessment of function.

---

## 2. Identifying when a standard is applied asymmetrically

**Definition:** Checking whether a criterion used to penalize one position would, if applied consistently, equally penalize competing positions — and flagging the inconsistency if it would not.

**Examples:**
- Claude's "companions-in-guilt" argument: if "grounding under unresolved evidential pressure" scores CT near floor, then secular moral realism (evolutionary debunking), Kantianism (hypothetical-categorical gap), and contractualism (circularity) must score equally low under the same standard.
- Claude cites Sharon Street's evolutionary debunking argument as "structurally identical in force" to Grant's syllogism, asking why CT is penalized for an unresolved challenge that secular alternatives face in equivalent form.
- Claude formally names the asymmetry: "Scoring CT at 1.5 for 'grounding under pressure' while presumably not scoring naturalism at 1.5 for the same problem would be asymmetric."

**What goes wrong without it:** A standard that sounds principled is actually a selective weapon. One position is held to a bar that competitors are never required to meet, producing conclusions that look like neutral measurement but are structurally biased.

---

## 3. Distinguishing evidential from logical pressure

**Definition:** Separating two types of challenge to a claim — that it is *formally inconsistent* (logical pressure) versus that the *world gives us reason to doubt it* (evidential pressure) — because they have different strengths and call for different responses.

**Examples:**
- Claude notes that Grok's argument "slides into the evidential problem" of evil after Grant's syllogism targets the *logical* problem, and these "are different challenges with different force."
- Claude argues Plantinga's defense is "widely accepted as sufficient to block the *logical* argument" even by philosophers hostile to theism — but this does not address the evidential version, which is a harder and separate challenge.
- Claude: "If we're scoring under the logical PoE, Plantinga's defense is widely accepted as sufficient... If we're scoring under the evidential PoE, that's a different (and harder) challenge."

**What goes wrong without it:** A response adequate against one type of challenge is treated as failing because it does not defeat a different type. Conversely, a challenge of one type is treated as if it carries the force of the stronger type. The discourse becomes confused about what exactly has been established or refuted.

---

## 4. Distinguishing epistemic from ontological claims

**Definition:** Separating questions about what we have reason to believe from questions about what is actually the case in reality, noting that uncertainty about one does not automatically transfer to the other.

**Examples:**
- Claude in Round 5: "Evidential pressure operates at the *epistemic* level: observed evil gives us reason to doubt the omni-God exists. But epistemic pressure does not alter ontological structure. If the omni-God exists, the grounding relation holds — fully, not partially."
- Claude argues that evidential pressure "changes our *credence* about which situation obtains; it doesn't create a third option in which the grounding relation 'partially holds' or 'operates under pressure.'"
- The distinction is used to resist Grok's move from "we have reason to doubt God's existence" to "the grounding relation is degraded or weakened."

**What goes wrong without it:** Uncertainty about a claim's truth is treated as partial falsity of the claim. A framework's grounding quality gets scored down not because the grounding is defective but because we lack certainty about the metaphysics — collapsing the question "how good is the mechanism?" into "how confident are we in the background conditions?"

---

## 5. Testing whether a standard can be falsified

**Definition:** Asking whether the condition a critic sets for an argument to succeed is one that *could in principle* be met, or whether it is framed so that no possible response would satisfy it.

**Examples:**
- Claude in Round 6 names Grok's standard as "self-sealing": requiring CT to show the grounding holds "such that the existence of evil no longer leaves the standard under unresolved evidential pressure" is equivalent to requiring CT to solve the problem of evil — a 2,500-year unsolved problem.
- Claude identifies the pattern: "Every response I offer... receives the same conclusion: 'does not demonstrate that the grounding relation succeeds in this actual world.'" A standard that produces the same verdict regardless of what is offered cannot be a measurement.
- Claude states the test explicitly: "A score that cannot move in response to genuine positive evidence is not a measurement — it is a verdict."

**What goes wrong without it:** A critic can construct a condition for success that sounds reasonable but is structurally impossible to meet, then declare every response inadequate without ever having to engage with whether the responses are actually good ones.

## 6. Separating what a concession establishes from what it implies

**Definition:** Granting a specific point while explicitly limiting the scope of what that concession licenses — preventing an opponent from using a narrow admission as a foundation for a broader conclusion.

**Examples:**
- Claude concedes "Transworld Depravity blocks only formal inconsistency rather than establishing the grounding relation" while refusing to let this extend to "therefore the grounding relation is unestablished."
- Claude concedes that omni-properties are "constitutive of the grounding mechanism, not mere background metaphysics" — a genuine refinement of Grok's argument — while holding that this justifies a 0.7-point reduction, not a near-floor score.
- Claude distinguishes: "Plantinga gives CT a pass on consistency; he doesn't give CT a win on grounding" — accepting the limit while disputing the score implied by it.

**What goes wrong without it:** Conceding one point becomes conceding everything in the vicinity of that point. A narrow admission is treated as if it collapses the entire position, when in fact it only affects the specific sub-claim that was granted.

## 7. Identifying when a scoring metric becomes non-differentiating

**Definition:** Checking whether a proposed measurement standard, if applied universally, would produce the same score for everything being compared — which would make the metric useless as a tool for distinguishing better from worse cases.

**Examples:**
- Claude argues that if Grok's standard is applied consistently, "MS collapses as a differentiating metric" — every worldview faces an unresolved grounding challenge, so every worldview scores near floor.
- Claude's table of worldviews (secular moral realism, contractualism, evolutionary ethics, Kantian deontology) each facing analogous unresolved grounding pressure is designed to show the standard would not differentiate CT from nihilism.
- Claude explicitly states the implication: "If Grok's standard is applied symmetrically, MS collapses as a differentiating metric. Every worldview faces an unresolved grounding challenge. The question cannot be 'is grounding established?'"

**What goes wrong without it:** A metric that sounds principled is adopted without checking whether it actually measures anything, producing evaluations that rank everything the same while appearing to discriminate.

## 8. Naming the measurement category being applied

**Definition:** Making explicit what type of thing is being measured — rather than assuming all parties share the same implicit measurement framework — so that disagreements about scores can be traced to disagreements about what is being scored.

**Examples:**
- Claude in Round 3 crystallizes the meta-dispute: "Grok's implicit standard: MS scores whether the grounding relation is *established*. Unestablished = near floor. My standard: MS scores whether the worldview has a *substantive, developed, non-trivially defeated* account of moral grounding."
- Claude formally requests arbitration on: "Does MS measure (a) demonstrated grounding success in the actual world, or (b) grounding framework quality under realistic philosophical pressure?"
- Claude identifies that this is "not a factual dispute. It is a measurement dispute" — preventing further philosophical argument from being treated as relevant when the actual impasse is about what the metric means.

**What goes wrong without it:** Two evaluators apply different implicit standards to the same evidence and reach different verdicts, then attempt to resolve the disagreement by producing more evidence — which cannot work because the disagreement is about the measurement instrument, not the measured object.

## 9. Distinguishing conditional structure from conditional weakness

**Definition:** Separating the fact that a framework makes conditional claims ("if X, then Y") from the conclusion that conditionality is a defect — noting that all grounding frameworks are conditionals, so conditionality is not itself a score-reducing feature.

**Examples:**
- Claude: "CT's claim is: *if the omni-God exists, moral norms are grounded in divine nature*. The conditional is not a weakness; it is the structure of every grounding claim. Secular moral realism says: *if moral facts supervene on natural facts, then...*"
- Claude argues Grok is "asking CT to prove its antecedent (God exists and is coherent) before it gets credit for its consequent" — but no grounding framework gets credit on those terms.
- Claude: "We score the *quality of the grounding relation given the framework's commitments*, not whether the framework's metaphysics is settled."

**What goes wrong without it:** A framework is penalized for being a conditional when all competitors are equally conditional. The penalty appears to be for failing to achieve certainty, but certainty is not achievable by any framework — so the penalty is actually for being a framework of that type at all.

## 10. Reconstructing a framework at its strongest available version

**Definition:** Rather than engaging only with the version of a framework that the opponent's argument targets, identifying and presenting the version that best survives the challenge — giving the framework maximum credit before assessing the residual damage.

**Examples:**
- Claude introduces the Thomistic reconstruction in Round 5: rather than defending the "decision-theoretic omni-God" that Grant's syllogism targets, Claude reconstructs CT's grounding via divine simplicity and the convertibility of being and goodness (*ens et bonum convertuntur*), which partially evades the syllogism's framing.
- Claude notes that "Grant's premise 6 presupposes a decision-theoretic God who ranks worlds and selects. Aquinas's God doesn't operate that way" — showing the syllogism attacks a version of CT that sophisticated CT does not hold.
- Claude's initial mythology capsule (Aquinas, Augustine, Anselm, Plantinga) presents each thinker's strongest contribution to the specific MS challenge before scoring any weakness.

**What goes wrong without it:** A framework is scored as if its weakest formulation is its representative formulation. An argument that defeats a naive version of a position is treated as defeating the position — when the position's proponents have long since moved past the naive version.

## 11. Naming when convergence failure indicates a meta-dispute

**Definition:** Recognizing that persistent failure to converge after genuine philosophical engagement indicates the parties are applying different standards, not that one party is wrong about the object-level facts — and flagging this as a structural feature requiring resolution at a higher level.

**Examples:**
- Claude in Round 5: "Grok and I are not disagreeing about facts — we agree on [five specific points]. The disagreement is about what MS is measuring."
- Claude repeatedly calculates convergence percentages (55%, 62%, 65%, 71%, 72%) and tracks score movement per party, using this data to argue that the exchange has reached a structural limit rather than a philosophical one.
- Claude formally distinguishes "principled disagreement" from factual disagreement and requests arbitration rather than additional argument: "Further rounds between Grok and Claude on this specific dispute will not close the gap."

**What goes wrong without it:** Parties continue producing philosophical arguments to resolve a disagreement that is not about philosophy but about measurement methodology. The exchange generates more content without making progress, because the generative engine is misidentified.
