# Catalog of Cognitive Operators

## 1. Exposing hidden epistemological assumptions

**Definition:** Surfacing an unstated theory of knowledge that an argument silently depends on, then questioning whether that theory is the only available option.

**Examples:**
- B identifies that A's argument presupposes a causal theory of knowledge: beliefs must be causally produced by what they track.
- B notes that this assumption is not self-evident — other epistemologies exist.
- B uses mathematical platonism as a counterexample to the assumption that causal contact is necessary for knowledge.

**Failure when absent:** The opponent's hidden framework becomes invisible. You rebut surface-level conclusions while the load-bearing premise — the epistemology — remains unexamined, so the argument regenerates indefinitely.

---

## 2. Constructing cross-domain structural analogies

**Definition:** Identifying a second domain facing the same structural challenge, noting that the challenge is considered solvable there, and transferring the solution template.

**Examples:**
- B maps moral facts onto mathematical facts: both causally inert, both potentially knowable.
- The platonist's toolkit — rational intuition, indispensability, structural correspondence — is offered as a transferable response template.
- The analogy establishes that A's challenge, if fatal to morality, should also be fatal to mathematics, which it isn't treated as.

**Failure when absent:** The opponent can present their challenge as uniquely devastating to one domain. Without a structural parallel, the challenge's real scope and force remain untested.

---

## 3. Tracking shifts in argumentative basis

**Definition:** Explicitly noting when an interlocutor has abandoned one line of argument and substituted a different one, preventing silent retreat.

**Examples:**
- B observes: "now you're arguing from utility, not causation" — the original causal inertness objection has been replaced.
- B does not let the shift go unremarked; the new argument is flagged as a different objection rather than a refinement.
- This prevents A from later claiming the causal argument still stands as a concurrent line of attack.

**Failure when absent:** Arguers drift from one basis to another without acknowledging it, creating the illusion of a single accumulating argument when it is actually a series of retractions and replacements.

---

## 4. Testing arguments by overextension

**Definition:** Applying the opponent's criterion to a case where they would reject the conclusion, showing the criterion proves too much.

**Examples:**
- A argues moral facts are suspect because they don't predict anything. B extends: aesthetic facts don't predict bridge loads either.
- The criterion "doesn't predict anything → doesn't exist" would also eliminate aesthetic facts A likely accepts.
- B does not argue aesthetics and morality are identical — only that A's criterion cannot discriminate between them.

**Failure when absent:** An argument seems strong because it is only ever applied to the target the opponent wants to discredit. Its destructive scope remains hidden, making it look like a scalpel when it functions as a bomb.

---

## 5. Distinguishing preference expression from fact tracking

**Definition:** Explicitly separating two activities that produce similar surface behavior — expressing attitudes versus detecting independent truths — and asking which is occurring.

**Examples:**
- A self-identifies: "I think I'm expressing preferences, not tracking mind-independent facts."
- This separates the phenomenology of moral discourse (which looks like fact-tracking) from the underlying activity A claims is occurring.
- B will later challenge whether this separation is stable by pressing on moral progress.

**Failure when absent:** Participants talk past each other — one treats moral language as descriptive, the other as expressive, and neither notices they are operating on different assumptions about what kind of activity is happening.

---

## 6. Treating phenomenology as explanatory target

**Definition:** Taking the first-person experience of an activity as data that any theory must account for, rather than dismissing it as noise.

**Examples:**
- B cites "the sense that we're discovering something rather than inventing it" as evidence.
- B does not claim the phenomenology is veridical — only that it is a fact requiring explanation, "even if the explanation turns out to be deflationary."
- The phenomenology of moral reasoning is treated as constraining the space of acceptable theories, not as irrelevant.

**Failure when absent:** A theory can explain away the experiential dimension by ignoring it. The result is a theory that is technically consistent but fails to account for the thing it purports to describe — like a theory of vision that never mentions seeing.

---

## 7. Confronting opponents with their own commitments

**Definition:** Showing that the opponent's stated position conflicts with beliefs they actually hold and will not abandon, forcing them to either revise or bite an implausible bullet.

**Examples:**
- B argues: if there are no moral facts, abolishing slavery is just a preference change. "But you don't actually believe that."
- B uses an extreme case (slavery) to test whether A's expressivist position is one A genuinely accepts across all applications.
- The move is not to prove slavery abolition is progress — it is to show A cannot consistently maintain their framework in practice.

**Failure when absent:** An opponent maintains a position that works in the abstract but collapses under concrete application. Without confronting them with cases they care about, the position appears stable when it is actually held in place by selective attention.
