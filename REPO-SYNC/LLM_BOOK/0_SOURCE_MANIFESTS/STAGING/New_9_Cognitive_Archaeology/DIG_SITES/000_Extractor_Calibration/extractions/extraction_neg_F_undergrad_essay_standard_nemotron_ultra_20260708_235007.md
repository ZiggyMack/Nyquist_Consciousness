**Catalog of Reasoning Operators**

### 1. Testing Principles with Counterfactual Cases
**Definition:** Constructing or invoking a hypothetical scenario that isolates a principle's implication in a sharp, concrete situation to reveal whether the principle yields a verdict that conflicts with a stable intuition. The case functions as a diagnostic probe, not an empirical prediction.

**Examples from text:**
- The trolley problem is invoked to show that utilitarianism "could justify harming one person to benefit many," exposing a clash with justice intuitions.
- Nozick's Experience Machine is deployed to test whether "people value more than just subjective experience," challenging the hedonistic assumption.
- Singer's drowning child argument makes the demandingness problem "vivid" by presenting a concrete, low-cost rescue case that utilitarianism says is obligatory.

**When absent:** Principles are evaluated only in the abstract or against other principles, never stress-tested against the specific verdicts they generate. The theory becomes unfalsifiable by intuition, and hidden contradictions remain dormant until they produce unacceptable real-world prescriptions.

---

### 2. Refining a Quantitative Metric with Qualitative Hierarchies
**Definition:** Responding to a counterexample that exploits a theory's single-dimensional metric (e.g., quantity of pleasure) by introducing a non-quantitative ranking (e.g., higher vs. lower pleasures) that reclassifies the inputs so the offending implication no longer follows.

**Examples from text:**
- Mill distinguishes "higher and lower pleasures, arguing that intellectual pleasures are qualitatively superior to bodily ones" to blunt the justice objection: a harm that produces only lower pleasures may not outweigh a higher-pleasure loss.
- The move implicitly reweights the calculus so that certain harms (to dignity, intellect) count more than the raw number of pleased bodies would suggest.

**When absent:** A single-scale metric (utils, dollars, preference-satisfaction) forces all values into commensurability. The theory then cannot block repugnant trades (e.g., torturing one for the amusement of many) because the metric has no internal resources to say some goods are incommensurably more important.

### 3. Deriving Extreme Demands to Test Structural Plausibility
**Definition:** Taking a principle's universal quantifier ("always maximize") literally and tracing its requirement across ordinary life to show that compliance would consume the agent's entire existence, thereby revealing the principle as incompatible with having a life of one's own.

**Examples from text:**
- "If we must always maximize utility, we can never rest while others suffer" — the demandingness problem extracted from the universalizing structure of the principle.
- Singer's drowning child extends the derivation: if distance doesn't matter, the obligation to prevent suffering is global and perpetual.

**When absent:** A principle is judged only by its verdicts in paradigmatic cases. Its totalizing force in non-paradigmatic, everyday situations goes unexamined, so the theory escapes the objection that it leaves no room for personal projects, relationships, or integrity.

---

### 4. Weighing Theoretical Virtues Against Intuitive Costs
**Definition:** Explicitly registering a theory's formal advantages (clarity, determinacy, unified criterion) and setting them against the accumulated weight of its counterintuitive implications, rather than treating either side as decisive alone.

**Examples from text:**
- The conclusion notes utilitarianism "provides a clear decision procedure" (virtue) but "its implications often conflict with our moral intuitions about rights, justice, and personal integrity" (costs).
- The evaluation does not declare the theory false because of the conflicts, nor does it dismiss the conflicts because of the decision procedure; it presents the tension as the live issue.

**When absent:** Evaluation collapses into either "this theory is great because it gives answers" (ignoring the answers' moral quality) or "this theory is terrible because it gives this one bad answer" (ignoring whether any alternative avoids similar problems). No comparative assessment is possible.

---

### 5. Contrasting Consequentialist and Deontic Structures
**Definition:** Identifying the deep architectural difference between a theory that evaluates acts by downstream outcomes and one that evaluates acts by intrinsic features (rules, rights, duties), and using that contrast to locate the source of intuitive resistance.

**Examples from text:**
- The opening notes utilitarianism "focuses on outcomes rather than abstract rules," framing its intuitive appeal as a structural virtue.
- The justice and rights objections are implicitly framed as the reappearance of deontic constraints that the consequentialist structure deliberately excluded.

**When absent:** Objections are treated as a laundry list of isolated counterexamples rather than symptoms of a single structural choice. The theorist cannot see that modifying one implication (e.g., adding a rights rider) may undermine the very structural feature (pure outcome-maximization) that made the theory attractive.

---

### 6. Exposing an Unstated Bridge Premise in a Dilemma
**Definition:** Identifying the hidden assumption that connects a principle's explicit formulation to a contested implication, so that the opponent must either defend the bridge premise or abandon the implication.

**Examples from text:**
- The demandingness objection relies on the bridge premise: "maximizing utility requires continuous sacrifice whenever net utility can be increased." The text makes this premise visible by stating "if we must always maximize utility, we can never rest while others suffer."
- The Experience Machine objection relies on the bridge premise: "if hedonism is true, a life in the machine is at least as good as a life outside." Nozick forces the hedonist to either accept the machine or reject the premise that only experience matters.

**When absent:** The implication is attributed directly to the principle, making the principle seem solely responsible. The defender cannot concede the implication without abandoning the principle, and the critic cannot target the specific auxiliary assumption that does the real work. The debate stalls at "intuition vs. theory" instead of locating the precise joint where revision could occur.
