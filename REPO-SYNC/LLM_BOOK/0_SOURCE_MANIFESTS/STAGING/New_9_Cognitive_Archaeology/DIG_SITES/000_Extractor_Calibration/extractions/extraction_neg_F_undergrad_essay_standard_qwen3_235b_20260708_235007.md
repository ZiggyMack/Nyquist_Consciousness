**Catalog of Reusable Reasoning Operators**

---

**1. Distinguishing outcomes from rules**  
*Definition:* Separating ethical evaluation based on consequences (consequentialism) from evaluation based on adherence to duties or principles (deontology).  
*Examples:*  
- The text contrasts utilitarianism’s focus on “outcomes” with “abstract rules,” highlighting a foundational divide in moral reasoning.  
- The trolley problem is used to show how rule-based intuitions (e.g., “don’t harm innocents”) conflict with outcome-based justification (e.g., “save more lives”).  
- The appeal to “moral intuitions about rights, justice, and personal integrity” invokes non-consequentialist standards that prioritize rules or constraints over results.  
*What goes wrong without it:* Reasoning collapses into a single evaluative mode, making it impossible to articulate why some beneficial actions still feel morally wrong. Moral discourse becomes rigid or blind to important dimensions of judgment.

---

**2. Introducing thought experiments to test intuitions**  
*Definition:* Constructing hypothetical scenarios designed to elicit intuitive reactions that challenge or refine a theory.  
*Examples:*  
- The trolley problem is invoked to test whether maximizing utility justifies active harm.  
- Nozick’s Experience Machine is cited to probe whether subjective pleasure alone constitutes well-being.  
- Singer’s drowning child scenario illustrates the extreme implications of utilitarian demands in everyday life.  
*What goes wrong without it:* Theoretical claims remain untested against lived values; abstract principles may become disconnected from human concerns. Moral reasoning lacks empirical grounding in psychological reality.

---

**3. Identifying counterexamples to a principle**  
*Definition:* Presenting specific cases where a general rule leads to intuitively unacceptable results, thereby challenging its validity.  
*Examples:*  
- The trolley problem serves as a counterexample to utilitarianism by suggesting it could demand unjustified harm.  
- The Experience Machine functions as a counterexample to hedonistic utilitarianism by showing people prefer reality over optimized experience.  
- The demandingness problem acts as a counterexample to universal utility maximization by implying moral exhaustion.  
*What goes wrong without it:* Principles are accepted uncritically even when they generate absurd or repugnant outcomes. Error detection in reasoning systems fails.

---

**4. Attributing qualitative distinctions within a quantitative framework**  
*Definition:* Introducing non-additive, hierarchical differences (e.g., “higher” vs. “lower”) into a system that otherwise treats values as commensurable and summable.  
*Examples:*  
- Mill distinguishes “higher and lower pleasures,” claiming intellectual pleasures are *qualitatively* superior, not just more intense.  
- This move attempts to prevent utilitarianism from equating base satisfaction with meaningful fulfillment.  
- Implicitly challenges the assumption that all utility can be measured on a single scale.  
*What goes wrong without it:* The theory risks trivializing human experience by reducing all value to a common metric (e.g., pleasure units), leading to implausible trade-offs.

---

**5. Appealing to moral intuitions as data**  
*Definition:* Treating common or widespread moral feelings not as conclusions, but as evidence to be explained or reconciled by a theory.  
*Examples:*  
- The claim that utilitarian implications “conflict with our moral intuitions about rights” treats those intuitions as inputs to theory evaluation.  
- The use of the Experience Machine relies on the intuition that plugging in would be wrong, even if pleasurable.  
- The trolley problem leverages the intuition that pushing someone to their death is worse than allowing death indirectly.  
*What goes wrong without it:* Theories become unmoored from human concerns, risking detachment from practical ethics. Intuition-free reasoning may produce “correct” but alienating prescriptions.

---

**6. Highlighting internal tension within a theory**  
*Definition:* Pointing out that a framework generates conflicting prescriptions or undermines its own assumptions under realistic conditions.  
*Examples:*  
- The demandingness problem reveals tension between utilitarianism’s universal scope and feasible human agency.  
- The justice objection shows that maximizing aggregate good may violate procedural fairness, undermining social trust—a potential precondition for long-term utility.  
- Mill’s qualitative hierarchy introduces a non-utilitarian criterion (kind over amount) that may not be justifiable within strict consequentialism.  
*What goes wrong without it:* Contradictions are ignored, allowing theories to persist despite self-undermining implications. Coherence checks fail.

---

**7. Separating theoretical clarity from practical acceptability**  
*Definition:* Acknowledging that a framework may be logically coherent or decision-procedurally clear while still being morally unacceptable in practice.  
*Examples:*  
- The conclusion states utilitarianism “provides a clear decision procedure” but “its implications often conflict with our moral intuitions,” separating structural virtue from normative plausibility.  
- Singer’s argument is logically compelling but practically overwhelming—highlighting the gap between clean logic and lived ethics.  
- Mill’s revisions suggest that raw utilitarianism needs supplementation to be palatable, implying clarity alone is insufficient.  
*What goes wrong without it:* Theoretical elegance is mistaken for truth or desirability. Systems are adopted not because they work, but because they are simple or tractable.

---

**8. Invoking a bridge premise to extend a theory**  
*Definition:* Introducing an implicit assumption that connects a descriptive claim (e.g., “this maximizes utility”) to a normative one (e.g., “this ought to be done”).  
*Examples:*  
- The assumption that “greatest good for the greatest number” should *determine right action* is not proven but presupposed.  
- Singer’s drowning child argument assumes that physical proximity doesn’t affect moral obligation—a hidden premise linking spatial facts to ethical duties.  
- The move from “pleasure is good” to “only pleasure matters” smuggles in a completeness claim not justified by observation.  
*What goes wrong without it:* Normative conclusions appear to follow from descriptive premises without justification (naturalistic fallacy risk). Logical gaps go unnoticed.

---

**9. Refining a theory in response to counterexamples**  
*Definition:* Modifying a framework to accommodate problematic cases while preserving its core logic.  
*Examples:*  
- Mill’s distinction between higher and lower pleasures is a refinement to address concerns that utilitarianism degrades human dignity.  
- Rule utilitarianism (implied by the justice critique) would modify act-based calculation to avoid repeated injustice.  
- Indirect strategies (e.g., following rules that usually maximize utility) respond to the demandingness problem.  
*What goes wrong without it:* Theories remain brittle and collapse under pressure from edge cases. Adaptive reasoning fails; dogmatism prevails.

---

**10. Contrasting intuitive appeal with critical scrutiny**  
*Definition:* Acknowledging initial plausibility of a view while subjecting it to deeper analysis that may undermine it.  
*Examples:*  
- The text notes utilitarianism is “intuitively appealing” due to its outcome focus, but then subjects it to objections that challenge that appeal.  
- The initial simplicity of “greatest good” is contrasted with the complexity of its real-world implications.  
- Singer’s argument feels compelling at first glance, but reflection reveals its radical demands.  
*What goes wrong without it:* First-order intuitions dominate discourse; critical thinking stops at surface plausibility. Cognitive ease overrides rigor.
