# Catalog of Reasoning Operators

## 1. Identifying hidden assumption in opponent's argument
**Definition:** Calling out an unstated premise on which the opponent's inference depends but does not defend.

**Examples from the text:**
- B's "You're assuming causal theories of knowledge are the only game in town." — B names the epistemological framework (causal epistemology) that A's inference silently rests on.
- A's implicit premise that knowledge requires a causal pathway from fact to belief is named by B via the parallel to mathematics.

**What goes wrong when absent:** The opponent's argument is contested only at its surface conclusion, while the load-bearing joint remains unchallenged. The same argument can then be re-launched indefinitely by retreating to the unstated premise.

---

## 2. Extending argument by parallel case across domains
**Definition:** Showing that an objection aimed at one domain applies equally to a domain whose status the interlocutor already accepts.

**Examples from the text:**
- B invokes mathematical knowledge: "Mathematical facts are also causally inert, yet we have mathematical knowledge."
- B later extends the utility criterion to aesthetics: "aesthetic facts don't predict bridge loads either, but you presumably think some paintings are better than others."

**What goes wrong when absent:** The interlocutor treats their target domain as uniquely problematic when the difficulty is generic. The objection is answered (or deflected) without recognizing it cuts against accepted beliefs too.

---

## 3. Tracking argumentative drift across dimensions
**Definition:** Noting that the opponent has shifted the type of objection being deployed (e.g., from causal to utility-based) and refusing to let one rebuttal cover both.

**Examples from the text:**
- B: "That's a different objection — now you're arguing from utility, not causation."
- B's earlier refusal to let the causal-inertness challenge be answered by the utility distinction (the two objections must be addressed separately).

**What goes wrong when absent:** The opponent can migrate between incompatible objections, and any single rebuttal is treated as disposing of the whole case. The interlocutor ends up arguing against a moving target.

---

## 4. Showing the argument proves too much
**Definition:** Extending an opponent's own principle to a case where they reject its conclusion, exposing overgeneralization.

**Examples from the text:**
- If "must predict something physical to be real" is the criterion, then aesthetic facts fail — but B assumes aesthetic facts are real, so the criterion is too strict.
- Implicitly: if causally inert facts cannot be known, mathematical knowledge is also impossible — yet A presumably accepts it.

**What goes wrong when absent:** One endorses a principle that, applied consistently, refutes beliefs one actually holds. The principle gets to do unearned work in the disputed case while being ignored elsewhere.

---

## 5. Separating ontology from epistemology
**Definition:** Distinguishing the question of whether a kind of thing exists from the question of how we would access or know about it.

**Examples from the text:**
- A's opening: "If moral facts exist, they must be causally inert" — granting metaphysical possibility, then posing the epistemological problem.
- B's parallel in mathematics preserves the separation: platonists about math accept inert ontology and develop independent epistemology.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Existence and accessibility get conflated. A thing's failure to be epistemically transparent is treated as a reason to deny its existence, or its existence is treated as automatically guaranteeing knowledge of it.

---

## 6. Requiring framework to accommodate counterdata
**Definition:** Pointing to a phenomenon the opponent's view seems to exclude, then demanding the framework explain rather than dismiss it.

**Examples from the text:**
- B: "If there are no moral facts, abolishing slavery isn't 'progress' — it's just a preference change. But you don't actually believe that."
- B frames moral progress as data that anti-realism must absorb or explain away.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Theoretical frameworks can quietly refuse to engage with phenomena they cannot accommodate, treating silence as victory. The framework becomes unfalsifiable by avoidance.

---

## 7. Using phenomenology as evidence
**Definition:** Treating the texture of first-person experience (the "what it's like") as data a theory must account for, distinct from the theory's verdict on its accuracy.

**Examples from the text:**
- B: "The phenomenology of moral reasoning — the sense that we're discovering something rather than inventing it — is evidence that needs explaining."
- B's insistence that the phenomenology must be addressed "even if the explanation turns out to be deflationary."

**What goes wrong when absent:** Subjective experience is treated as either irrelevant (epiphenomenal) or automatically veridical. A theory can either ignore it or be hijacked by it; either way, the data is not engaged on its own terms.

---

## 8. Distinguishing preference-expression from fact-tracking
**Definition:** Separating the act of registering or expressing a mental state from the act of correctly representing an external, mind-independent reality.

**Examples from the text:**
- A: "I think I'm expressing preferences, not tracking mind-independent facts."
- The implicit contrast: moral judgment could be a state of the speaker, or a representational relation to a fact.

**What goes wrong when absent:** The distinction between "I feel that P" and "P is true" collapses. Either every felt conviction is taken to track truth, or no felt conviction is — both moves lose the intermediate possibility of error and the structure of representational content.

---

## 9. Identifying unstated bridge premise
**Definition:** Naming the inferential link between premises that the argument assumes but does not articulate.

**Examples from the text:**
- A's inference depends on the bridge premise: knowledge of X requires that X causally influence the knower. A states "causally inert moral realm has no pathway to influence belief formation" — the bridge is the assumption that causal contact is necessary for knowledge.
- A's later bridge premise: "real" status requires empirical predictive utility. The bridge between "predicts nothing" and "therefore not real" is left implicit.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Arguments rest on invisible inferential steps. The interlocutor can be moved by claims they would reject if the bridge were made explicit, and no one notices the inferential gap.

---

## 10. Separating framework's claim from framework's success
**Definition:** Acknowledging that a framework is making a substantive claim while remaining neutral on whether the claim is correct or vindicated.

**Examples from the text:**
- B allows that the explanation of moral phenomenology "turns out to be deflationary" — granting the realist framework's claim to be considered without committing to its success.
- A's anti-realist framing is treated as a position to engage rather than a settled conclusion.

**What goes wrong when absent:** The framework is evaluated before being understood (rejected as silly), or accepted because its existence feels substantive. The two steps — articulating what a view claims and assessing whether it delivers — get fused.

---

## 11. Arguing from empirical track record against principled objection
**Definition:** Using the practical success of a domain to deflect a principled (often a priori) objection to its cognitive status.

**Examples from the text:**
- A: "Math earns its keep through empirical utility. Moral facts don't predict anything." — invoking math's track record to mark its epistemic standing.
- The implicit asymmetry: math is vindicated by application; morality is not.

**What goes wrong when absent:** Domains without obvious predictive utility are dismissed as cognitively defective. Track record becomes the sole arbiter of cognitive legitimacy, ruling out any inquiry whose practical yield is invisible.
