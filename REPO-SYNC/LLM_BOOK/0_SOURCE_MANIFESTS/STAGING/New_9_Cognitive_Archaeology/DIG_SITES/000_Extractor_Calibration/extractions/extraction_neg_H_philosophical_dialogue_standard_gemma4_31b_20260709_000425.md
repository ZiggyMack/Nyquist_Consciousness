### Catalog of Reasoning Operators

**1. Identify hidden assumptions**
*   **Definition:** Explicitly naming a premise that the opponent is relying upon but has not stated, thereby making the assumption a target for critique.
*   **Examples:** 
    *   "You're assuming causal theories of knowledge are the only game in town."
*   **Failure Mode:** The argument proceeds on a foundation that is never questioned, allowing a narrow or flawed framework to be accepted as the only possible lens.

**2. Test via parallel domains**
*   **Definition:** Applying the opponent's logic to a different subject area (an isomorphism) to see if the logic holds or if it creates an impossible contradiction in a domain the opponent accepts.
*   **Examples:** 
    *   Comparing the causal inertness of moral facts to the causal inertness of mathematical facts.
    *   Comparing the empirical utility of moral facts to that of aesthetic facts.
*   **Failure Mode:** A logic that seems sound in one specific context is never checked for universal consistency, allowing "special pleading" to go unnoticed.

**3. Identify a shift in criteria**
*   **Definition:** Noting when an interlocutor changes the basis of their objection or the standard of evidence they are using mid-argument.
*   **Examples:** 
    *   "That's a different objection — now you're arguing from utility, not causation."
*   **Failure Mode:** The dialogue becomes a "moving target" where the original problem is never solved, but the speaker feels they are winning because they are successfully attacking a new, unrelated point.

**4. Demonstrate a criterion "proves too much"**
*   **Definition:** Showing that a proposed rule or requirement for truth/existence, if applied consistently, would eliminate things that the opponent actually believes to be true.
*   **Examples:** 
    *   Using the lack of "bridge-building utility" to show that if utility is required for facts, then aesthetic facts must also be rejected.
*   **Failure Mode:** Overly restrictive criteria are accepted, leading to an unintentional "scorched earth" result where the speaker accidentally deletes their own beliefs.

**5. Challenge the intelligibility of implications**
*   **Definition:** Forcing an opponent to explain how a common experience or concept (e.g., "progress") remains coherent if the opponent's proposed framework is true.
*   **Examples:** 
    *   "Then explain why moral progress is intelligible [if there are no moral facts]."
*   **Failure Mode:** A theory is accepted because it is logically tidy, even though it renders the actual lived experience of the world incomprehensible.

**6. Introduce phenomenological constraints**
*   **Definition:** Asserting that the internal, subjective experience of a process (the "feeling" of discovery vs. invention) constitutes data that any successful theory must account for.
*   **Examples:** 
    *   Citing "the phenomenology of moral reasoning — the sense that we're discovering something" as evidence that needs explaining.
*   **Failure Mode:** The reasoning remains purely abstract or theoretical, ignoring the "user experience" of the mind, which often reveals the flaws in the theoretical model.
