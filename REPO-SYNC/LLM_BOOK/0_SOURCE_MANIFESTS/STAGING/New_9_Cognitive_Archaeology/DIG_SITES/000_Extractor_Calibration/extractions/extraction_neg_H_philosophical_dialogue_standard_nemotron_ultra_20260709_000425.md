### Catalog of Reasoning Operators

---

#### 1. Exposing a Presupposed Epistemic Framework
**Definition:** Identifying and naming the background theory of knowledge or justification that an interlocutor treats as exhaustive, thereby revealing it as a contestable premise rather than a neutral starting point.  
**Examples:**  
- B: "You're assuming causal theories of knowledge are the only game in town."  
- B implicitly flags A’s move from "moral facts are causally inert" to "we cannot know them" as reliant on an unstated causal theory of knowledge.  
- B introduces platonist responses (rational intuition, indispensability, structural correspondence) as existing alternatives that A’s argument ignores.  
**Failure mode:** The challenger’s objection appears decisive only because the target has silently ruled out competing epistemic frameworks. The debate stalls on a premise neither party has defended.

---

#### 2. Mapping an Isomorphic Problem Across Domains
**Definition:** Translating a challenge raised in one domain (e.g., moral epistemology) into a structurally identical challenge in a second domain (e.g., mathematical epistemology) where the target already accepts knowledge is possible, thereby forcing consistency or a principled distinction.  
**Examples:**  
- B: "Mathematical facts are also causally inert, yet we have mathematical knowledge. The platonist about math faces the same challenge and has responses."  
- B treats the causal inertness → epistemic access gap as a single abstract structure instantiated in both math and morals.  
- B imports the platonist’s toolkit (rational intuition, indispensability) as a ready-made response set for the moral case.  
**Failure mode:** The target dismisses the analogy without articulating a relevant disanalogy, or accepts the analogy but fails to see that it shifts the burden to *them* to explain why the response works in one domain but not the other.

---

#### 3. Flagging an Objection-Switch
**Definition:** Noting that an interlocutor has abandoned their original line of attack and substituted a different one, often without acknowledging the shift, thereby evading the response to the first objection.  
**Examples:**  
- B: "That's a different objection — now you're arguing from utility, not causation."  
- A moves from "causal inertness blocks knowledge" to "moral facts lack empirical predictive utility."  
- B catches the switch in real time, preventing the discussion from collapsing into a moving target.  
**Failure mode:** The conversation becomes a game of whack-a-mole; each deflected objection is quietly replaced by another, and no single argument ever receives a sustained answer.

---

#### 4. Testing a View by Its Implications for Paradigm Cases
**Definition:** Deriving the consequences of a general thesis for specific, widely accepted judgments (e.g., "abolishing slavery was progress") to see whether the thesis renders those judgments unintelligible or false.  
**Examples:**  
- B: "If there are no moral facts, abolishing slavery isn't 'progress' — it's just a preference change. But you don't actually believe that."  
- B uses the paradigm case of slavery abolition as a fixed point that any adequate metaethical view must accommodate.  
- B infers that A’s anti-realism fails a adequacy condition: it cannot make sense of a moral judgment A themselves endorses.  
**Failure mode:** The view is evaluated only on abstract theoretical virtues (parsimony, naturalism) while its inability to recover ordinary moral discourse goes unnoticed. The theory "saves the appearances" only by denying them.

---

#### 5. Appealing to Phenomenology as Constraining Evidence
**Definition:** Treating the first-person character of a cognitive practice (e.g., the sense of discovery in moral reasoning) as a datum that any successful theory must explain, even if the ultimate explanation is deflationary.  
**Examples:**  
- B: "The phenomenology of moral reasoning — the sense that we're discovering something rather than inventing it — is evidence that needs explaining, even if the explanation turns out to be deflationary."  
- B does not claim the phenomenology *proves* realism; only that it is a phenomenon requiring an account.  
- B explicitly allows that the explanation could be deflationary (e.g., evolutionary illusion), but insists the explanandum is real.  
**Failure mode:** Theorists either (a) dismiss the phenomenology as mere illusion without explaining why the illusion arises, or (b) treat it as decisive proof of ontology, skipping the step of asking what *best* explains it.

---

#### 6. Blocking a Category-Mistake Objection via Overgeneralization
**Definition:** Showing that a criterion deployed against a target domain (e.g., "moral facts don’t predict bridge loads") also excludes a comparison domain the target accepts (e.g., aesthetic facts), forcing the target to either drop the criterion or accept the comparison domain’s exclusion.  
**Examples:**  
- B: "Aesthetic facts don't predict bridge loads either, but you presumably think some paintings are better than others."  
- B identifies "empirical predictive utility" as the criterion A is using.  
- B produces aesthetics as a domain A likely accepts as factual/normative but which fails the same criterion.  
- B forces a trilemma: abandon the criterion, deny aesthetic facts, or accept moral facts.  
**Failure mode:** The criterion is applied selectively, protecting favored domains while excluding disfavored ones, with no principled reason for the asymmetry.

---

#### 7. Separating Ontological Commitment from Epistemic Access
**Definition:** Distinguishing the question "Do X-facts exist?" from "How could we know X-facts?" and refusing to let an epistemic gap entail an ontological verdict.  
**Examples:**  
- B’s entire strategy: grants A’s premise that moral facts would be causally inert, but refuses the inference to "therefore we can’t know them" or "therefore they don’t exist."  
- B points to math: ontological commitment to abstract objects + epistemic access via non-causal routes.  
- B treats the causal inertness of moral facts as a *constraint on epistemology*, not a refutation of ontology.  
**Failure mode:** The epistemic challenge is treated as an ontological disproof. The target concludes "no moral facts" when the argument only supports "moral knowledge would require a non-causal epistemology."
