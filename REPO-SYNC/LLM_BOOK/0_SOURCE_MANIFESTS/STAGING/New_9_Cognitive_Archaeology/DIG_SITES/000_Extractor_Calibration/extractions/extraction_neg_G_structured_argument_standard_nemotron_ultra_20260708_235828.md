### 1. Exposing Structural Tension

**Definition:** Identifying two legitimate requirements or principles that pull in opposite directions such that satisfying one systematically undermines the other, revealing a built-in conflict rather than a contingent flaw.

**Examples from text:**
- Premise 1 (legitimacy requires meaningful evaluation) vs. Premise 2 (modern policy exceeds citizen expertise) — the author constructs the tension by showing each premise is independently plausible but jointly incompatible.
- The conclusion "democratic legitimacy faces a structural tension with technocratic governance" names the tension as structural (inescapable by design) rather than accidental.

**What goes wrong without it:** Conflicts get misdiagnosed as fixable errors. One side treats the tension as a bug to patch (e.g., "just educate voters better") while the other treats it as a feature to accept (e.g., "just defer to experts"), and the conversation talks past itself because the structural nature of the bind is never made explicit.

### 2. Demonstrating Problem Displacement

**Definition:** Showing that a proposed solution does not resolve the original problem but instead relocates it to a higher or prior level, preserving the same structural difficulty in a new guise.

**Examples from text:**
- Objection: "voters can delegate to trusted experts" → Rebuttal: "this merely relocates the problem — how does a non-expert evaluate which expert to trust?" The same epistemic asymmetry (non-expert judging expert) reappears at the meta-level.
- The delegation move is exposed as a shell game: the evaluation burden shifts from policy content to expert selection, but the voter's epistemic deficit remains.

**What goes wrong without it:** Solutions that merely displace problems get accepted as genuine resolutions. The discourse accumulates "solutions" that stack displacement layers (vote for representatives who appoint experts who hire consultants...) while the root epistemic gap goes unaddressed.

### 3. Surfacing Bridge Premises

**Definition:** Identifying an unstated premise that must hold for an argument's inference to go through, then evaluating whether that premise is defensible or merely assumed.

**Examples from text:**
- Landemore's cognitive diversity argument: "this assumes the aggregation mechanism (voting) preserves the information that diversity generates." The author extracts the hidden premise — that voting reliably aggregates dispersed information — which the diversity argument silently depends on.
- The further move: "If the aggregation mechanism is vulnerable to manipulation..., the diversity argument fails" — the bridge premise is not just identified but stress-tested.

**What goes wrong without it:** Arguments appear valid because their critical load-bearing premises are invisible. Critics attack the wrong level (e.g., disputing whether diversity exists) while the real vulnerability — the aggregation assumption — remains unexamined and unchallenged.

### 4. Stress-Testing Aggregation Mechanisms

**Definition:** Taking a collective process (voting, markets, deliberation) that is claimed to produce a desirable epistemic property (truth-tracking, error-cancellation, wisdom-of-crowds), and asking whether that property survives realistic distortions of the process.

**Examples from text:**
- The diversity argument claims large groups compensate for individual ignorance → Author asks: what if the aggregation mechanism (voting) is "vulnerable to manipulation (gerrymandering, media capture, algorithmic amplification)"? The claimed epistemic benefit is conditional on mechanism integrity.
- The move generalizes: any "crowd wisdom" claim must survive contact with the actual institutions that implement aggregation.

**What goes wrong without it:** Idealized models of collective intelligence are treated as guarantees. Real-world institutional pathologies (capture, distortion, strategic voting) are treated as "implementation details" rather than falsifiers of the epistemic claim.

### 5. Recentering Explanatory Locus

**Definition:** Shifting the primary explanatory variable from individual-level attributes (knowledge, competence, virtue) to structural or environmental conditions (institutional design, information ecology, incentive architecture).

**Examples from text:**
- Final sentence: "the quality of democratic institutions depends not on the epistemic capacity of individual voters but on the structural integrity of the information environment in which they operate." The locus moves from voter competence → information environment integrity.
- The move reframes the whole debate: improving voters is the wrong lever; protecting the information environment is the right one.

**What goes wrong without it:** Policy interventions target the wrong level. Efforts pour into civic education, fact-checking, or voter competence while the structural conditions that make competence irrelevant (or impossible to exercise) persist unchanged.

### 6. Blocking Sufficiency Claims

**Definition:** Showing that a proposed sufficient condition for a desired outcome actually depends on additional unmet conditions, thereby converting a claimed sufficiency into a mere necessity (or revealing it as insufficient altogether).

**Examples from text:**
- Cognitive diversity is presented as sufficient for good democratic epistemic outcomes → Author shows diversity only works *if* the aggregation mechanism preserves information → Diversity becomes necessary but not sufficient; mechanism integrity becomes the binding constraint.
- The pattern: "X solves Y" → "X solves Y *only if* Z holds" → "Z is the real bottleneck."

**What goes wrong without it:** Partial solutions are treated as complete. Resources flow into maximizing the proposed sufficient condition (more diversity! more participation!) while the actual binding constraint (aggregation integrity) is starved.

### 7. Recursive Application of a Critique

**Definition:** Applying the same reasoning pattern that generated a problem to the proposed solution itself, revealing that the solution inherits the original defect.

**Examples from text:**
- Original problem: non-experts cannot evaluate policy → Proposed solution: non-experts evaluate experts → Author applies the same epistemic asymmetry: non-experts cannot evaluate experts either.
- The critique "how does a non-expert evaluate which expert to trust?" is the original critique ("how does a non-expert evaluate policy?") with "policy" replaced by "expert."

**What goes wrong without it:** Infinite regresses of meta-solutions go undetected. Each new layer (meta-experts to choose experts, meta-meta-experts to choose meta-experts...) gets proposed in good faith because the recursive structure of the problem is never made visible.
