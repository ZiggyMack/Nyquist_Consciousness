### Catalog of Reusable Reasoning Operators

1. **Identifying hidden assumptions**
   - **Definition**: This operation involves uncovering underlying beliefs or premises that are not explicitly stated but are necessary for the argument to hold.
   - **Examples**: 
     - The assumption that maximizing utility inherently leads to a just outcome may not be conveyed directly but is essential to the utilitarian framework.
     - The implicit belief that all pleasures can be quantitatively measured before addressing the differentiation of pleasures.
   - **What goes wrong**: Without identifying these hidden assumptions, arguments may proceed based on flawed or unexamined premises, leading to unsupported conclusions.

2. **Distinguishing map from territory**
   - **Definition**: This operation involves recognizing the difference between theoretical models or frameworks and the actual real-world situations they aim to describe.
   - **Examples**:
     - The discussion of utilitarianism as a clear decision-making process contrasts with the real-life complexities of ethical dilemmas, like the trolley problem.
     - The bridge between the theoretical pleasure maximization and its practical implications reveals the limits of a purely utilitarian approach.
   - **What goes wrong**: Failing to make this distinction can result in applying a theory too rigidly to nuanced situations, leading to inappropriate or harmful decisions.

3. **Distinguishing descriptive from normative claims**
   - **Definition**: This operation involves differentiating between statements that describe the world and those that prescribe how it should be.
   - **Examples**:
     - Utilitarianism as a descriptive ethical theory versus individuals' normative beliefs about justice and rights.
     - Mill's categorization of pleasures can be seen as a descriptive claim about the nature of pleasure versus a normative stance on which pleasures should be valued more.
   - **What goes wrong**: Neglecting this distinction can cause confusion regarding what is being asserted and the implications of those assertions, leading to moral contradictions.

4. **Attacking a specific premise in an opponent's argument**
   - **Definition**: This operation involves directly critiquing a central idea or assumption that supports an opponent's argument.
   - **Examples**:
     - Nozick's Experience Machine challenges the premise that subjective pleasure is the only measure of a good life.
     - The demandingness problem critiques the notion that individuals are required to maximize utility at all times.
   - **What goes wrong**: Without targeting specific premises, the argument remains uncontested, and faulty logic may continue to mislead others.

5. **Distinguishing what a framework claims from whether the claim succeeds**
   - **Definition**: This operation involves evaluating the claims made by a conceptual framework against empirical evidence and real-world outcomes.
   - **Examples**:
     - While utilitarianism claims to optimize happiness, real-life applications may often conflict with established moral intuitions.
     - The implication that utilitarian decision-making can be universally applied is challenged by various ethical dilemmas.
   - **What goes wrong**: Ignoring this distinction can lead to an acceptance of frameworks that don’t actually work in practice, undermining effective moral deliberation.

6. **Reconstructing a framework more charitably than presented**
   - **Definition**: This operation involves interpreting an opponent's argument in the best possible light, even if it may not seem well-articulated at first.
   - **Examples**:
     - Supporting Mill's distinction of pleasures as an attempt to elevate the utilitarian argument to accommodate critiques about justice.
     - Understanding Singer's drowning child argument as an effort to illustrate a moral intuitiveness rather than to be taken as prescriptive.
   - **What goes wrong**: Without charitable reconstruction, valuable insights can be disregarded, and discussions can devolve into misunderstandings or misrepresentations of positions. 

7. **Appealing to empirical track record against logical objection**
   - **Definition**: This operation involves using empirical evidence to counteract purely logical or philosophical objections to a framework or argument.
   - **Examples**:
     - Citing real-world examples where utilitarian principles have led to beneficial social outcomes despite theoretical critiques.
     - Referencing historical trends in moral philosophy that align with utilitarian outcomes even when logic might suggest otherwise.
   - **What goes wrong**: When this operation is absent, discussions may become overly abstract and disconnected from practical realities, leading to ineffective moral philosophies.
