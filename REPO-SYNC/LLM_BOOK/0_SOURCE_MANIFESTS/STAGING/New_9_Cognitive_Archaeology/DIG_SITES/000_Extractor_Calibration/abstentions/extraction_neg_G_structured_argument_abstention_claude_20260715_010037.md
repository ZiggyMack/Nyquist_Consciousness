### TRUE OMISSIONS

**002 — Hidden Selection Audit** *(True Omission)*

The argument treats "democratic legitimacy" as having a fixed referent, but never asks: *which version of democratic legitimacy is being selected here, and by what mechanism?* The text quietly imports an *epistemic* conception of legitimacy (legitimacy requires correct policy evaluation) rather than, say, a *proceduralist* conception (legitimacy requires fair process regardless of outcome quality). This selection is doing enormous work — the entire tension only arises under the epistemic conception. Under proceduralism, Premise 3 simply doesn't generate a legitimacy problem.

**What would have changed:** The "structural tension" may dissolve or relocate entirely. The argument would need to defend its chosen conception of legitimacy before concluding there is a tension, rather than smuggling in the most demanding conception available.

---

**005 — Hidden Structure Injection** *(True Omission)*

Closely related but distinct from the above: the text injects an unstated *standard of meaningful evaluation* in Premise 1. What counts as "meaningful"? The argument treats this as obvious, but it is doing all the discriminating work. If "meaningful" means "technically accurate," the argument goes through. If "meaningful" means "value-responsive" (voters track what they care about, not technical details), the argument may not go through at all — voters might legitimately defer on means while retaining authority over ends.

**What would have changed:** The entire framing of the epistemic objection shifts. The text conflates epistemic competence over *means* with competence over *ends*, and flagging the injection would expose this equivocation.

---

**006 — Under-Determination Detection** *(True Omission)*

The conclusion ("democratic legitimacy depends on structural integrity of the information environment") is presented as following from the argument, but the argument underdetermines it. Multiple conclusions are compatible with the premises: (a) democracy should be constrained by technocratic override, (b) we should improve the information environment, (c) democratic legitimacy should be redefined. The text lands on (b)/(c) without acknowledging that the logical structure licenses all options equally.

**What would have changed:** The thinker would need to supply the extra structure (a normative preference, a pragmatic constraint) that selects the actual conclusion, making that selection visible and arguable rather than appearing to follow necessarily.

---

**011 — Subtractive Discovery** *(True Omission)*

The argument adds the Landemore objection and then adds the manipulation counter-objection, building complexity. It never asks: *what is the minimal set of conditions under which the tension disappears?* Running the argument in reverse — what single assumption, if removed, dissolves the problem? — would clarify whether the tension is structural or contingent. For instance, if Premise 1 is narrowed (legitimacy requires only *value* evaluation, not technical evaluation), the entire argument collapses gracefully.

**What would have changed:** Rather than arriving at a vague structural conclusion, the thinker might identify the precise load-bearing assumption, producing a more actionable diagnosis.
