### Operator-by-Operator Assessment

**001 | Representation ≠ Ontology** — RELEVANT
The entire dialogue concerns whether moral language *represents* mind-independent facts or merely expresses preferences. This operator is **partially used** by B ("expressing preferences, not tracking mind-independent facts") but never made fully explicit as a framing device. Competing priority: the causal pathway argument dominates attention.

**002 | Hidden Selection Audit** — RELEVANT, **TRUE OMISSION**
A's central argument assumes that *causal-contact* knowledge is the operative selection mechanism — i.e., the only way beliefs can be "about" something is if that something causally produces the belief. Nobody asks: *what selects this particular theory of knowledge acquisition?* The causal theory of knowledge is itself a contestable choice smuggled in as default. B gestures at this ("assuming causal theories are the only game") but doesn't name the selection mechanism or ask why it was privileged.

**What would have changed:** Explicitly auditing what selected the causal theory of knowledge as the standard would have revealed A's argument as question-begging against any rationalist or non-naturalist epistemology — not just moral realism. The debate would have shifted upstream to philosophy of knowledge itself, which is where it actually lives.

**003 | Goal → Optimization Collapse** — Not relevant. No strategic optimization structure present.

**004 | Reconstruction Before Judgment** — RELEVANT, **TRUE OMISSION**
A never faithfully reconstructs the moral realist position before attacking it. The strongest versions of moral realism (Cornell realism, non-naturalist intuitionism, quasi-realism) have detailed responses to the causal inertness objection that go unacknowledged. A treats a simplified target.

**What would have changed:** A would have had to engage with Huemer's phenomenal conservatism, Parfit's on what matters, or Street's evolutionary debunking — all of which reframe the epistemology question. The dialogue would have been more diagnostically precise rather than trading analogies.

**005 | Hidden Structure Injection** — RELEVANT, **TRUE OMISSION**
A's argument quietly imports: (1) physicalist ontology, (2) causal closure of the mental, (3) reliabilist epistemology. None of these are declared. They do enormous work but are invisible.

**What would have changed:** Making these imports explicit would have forced A to defend them or acknowledge that the argument is conditional on a contested philosophical package, not a knockdown objection to moral realism per se.

**006 | Under-Determination Detection** — RELEVANT, **USED**
B partially deploys this: "that's a different objection — now you're arguing from utility, not causation." B correctly detects that A's conclusion (no moral facts) is underdetermined by the causal argument alone — the utility argument is a *separate* premise being smuggled in. This is the most clearly executed operation in the text.

**007 | Locate Disagreement Layer** — RELEVANT, **TRUE OMISSION**
The interlocutors never identify *which layer* their disagreement inhabits. Is it:
- **Ontological** (do moral facts exist?)
- **Epistemological** (can we know them if they do?)
- **Semantic** (what does moral language do?)
- **Phenomenological** (what does moral reasoning feel like and what does that prove?)

They drift across all four layers without noticing. B's final move about "phenomenology of moral reasoning" is actually a layer-shift from ontology to phenomenology, unannounced.

**What would have changed:** Layer-locating early would have revealed they are often talking past each other — A's causal argument is epistemological, B's moral progress argument is semantic/ontological, and B's final move is phenomenological. Clarifying this would have sharpened every exchange.

**008 | Symmetry Testing of Standards** — RELEVANT, **USED (partially)**
B applies this well with the aesthetic facts analogy: "aesthetic facts don't predict bridge loads either." B tests whether A's utility standard, applied symmetrically, produces the desired discrimination — and shows it doesn't, because A presumably accepts aesthetic judgments. A's response (preferences, not facts) is a retreat, not a rebuttal.

**009 | Contested ≠ Defeated** — RELEVANT, **TRUE OMISSION**
A treats the causal inertness problem as if it *defeats* moral realism. B gestures at responses (rational intuition, indispensability arguments) but doesn't explicitly invoke the principle that a difficulty facing a position is not a refutation of it — especially when analogous difficulties face positions A presumably accepts (math, aesthetics).

**What would have changed:** Explicitly invoking this principle would have reframed the dialectic: A's argument establishes a *challenge* for moral realism, not a *refutation*. The burden of proof question (who must answer the challenge?) would then have become the live issue.

**010 | Altitude Escalation** — RELEVANT, **DELIBERATE REFUSAL (implicit)**
Neither party steps back to ask: *what kind of argument would settle this?* They remain at the object level throughout. This appears to be a deliberate feature of the dialogue format — staying in the dialectic rather than examining it.

**Failure mode avoided:** Premature meta-discussion that dissolves the substantive engagement before it develops.

**013 | Epistemic Boundary Setting** — RELEVANT, **TRUE OMISSION**
Neither party declares the limits of their own position. A never says: "I'm committed to physicalism and reliabilism — my argument only runs given those assumptions." B never says: "I'm not defending full-blown moral realism, just resisting the causal argument." Without these boundary declarations, it's unclear what each party is actually committed to defending.

**What would have changed:** The argument would have been conditionally scoped. A's objection would be clearly labeled as internal to a physicalist-reliabilist package; B's response would be clarified as defensive rather than asserting positive moral realism.

**014 | Ontological Downgrading** — RELEVANT, **TRUE OMISSION**
The dialogue treats "moral facts exist" as binary. But there's a graded spectrum: robust Platonist moral facts → quasi-realist constructions → response-dependent facts → purely expressivist accounts. B hints at this ("even if the explanation turns out to be deflationary") but neither party deploys the spectrum explicitly.

**What would have changed:** Much of A's objection targets robust Platonism. Ontological downgrading to quasi-realism or response-dependence might sidestep the causal inertness problem entirely while preserving the phenomenology B invokes. This move would have substantially altered the dialectic.

**015 | Question Completion** — RELEVANT, **TRUE OMISSION**
Neither party generates the higher-order questions that would maximally advance understanding. Examples never asked: *What would evidence for or against moral realism look like? What would it take to settle the causal theory of knowledge dispute? Is "moral progress" the right test case?*

**What would have changed:** The dialogue ends in a standoff. Question completion would have identified what the *next* productive move is — likely a detailed examination of the epistemology of mathematics as the real testing ground, since that's where the analogy is doing the most work.
