## Why Jaynes?

Jaynes spent his career arguing that probability is about INFERENCE, not physical reality — that confusion between epistemic probability and physical frequency is the root cause of most paradoxes in statistical mechanics and quantum foundations. This is structurally isomorphic to OP-001 (representation ≠ ontology) applied to probability itself. His maximum entropy program is OP-006 (under-determination detection) weaponized: given constraints, what is the LEAST committal distribution? Everything beyond that is smuggled assumption.

Barandes cites Jaynes in the ISP context — there's a direct intellectual lineage to test. Does Jaynes exhibit the same operators Barandes does, or different ones applied to the same domain?

## Recommended Sources (Priority Order)

### Tier 1: ESSENTIAL

**1. "Probability Theory: The Logic of Science" (2003)**
- Cambridge University Press — published posthumously, edited by Larry Bretthorst
- Jaynes' magnum opus — Chapters 1-2 (foundations) are the methodological core
- Chapter 15 ("Paradoxes of Probability Theory") is pure operator extraction gold
- The PREFACE by Bretthorst describes Jaynes' cognitive style
- Available legally online in draft form (Jaynes' website at WUSTL)

**2. "Where Do We Stand on Maximum Entropy?" (1978)**
- Conference paper — Jaynes defends his program against critics
- Adversarial context reveals operators under pressure
- Concise distillation of his methodology

**3. Jaynes' collected papers and lectures**
- Many available at bayes.wustl.edu (Washington University archives)
- His informal talks are more process-revealing than formal papers
- "The Mind Projection Fallacy" papers are particularly relevant — the fallacy IS an operator (OP-005 applied to probability)

### Tier 2: HIGH VALUE

**4. "Information Theory and Statistical Mechanics" (1957)**
- Physical Review — the founding paper of maximum entropy methods
- Shows the cognitive move IN ACTION: what does Jaynes DO when he formulates MaxEnt?
- Short but the methodology is explicit

**5. "Bayesian Methods: General Background" (1986)**
- Tutorial paper — pedagogical choices reveal architecture (tests OP-012)
- How Jaynes teaches probability reveals what he considers essential vs dispensable

**6. Interviews and lectures (if available)**
- Jaynes died in 1998 — fewer modern long-form interviews than other targets
- Check: recorded conference talks, WUSTL lecture recordings
- His writing style is conversational — papers partially compensate for lack of interviews

### Tier 3: BACKGROUND

**7. Cox's Theorem papers (R.T. Cox, 1946)**
- The mathematical foundation Jaynes builds on
- Understanding Cox helps identify which parts of Jaynes' architecture are inherited vs original

**8. Jaynes vs Frequentists debates**
- His arguments against frequentist probability
- Adversarial exchanges reveal which operators he deploys repeatedly
- The "Jaynes vs the world" dynamic parallels Barandes' stance against Copenhagen

**9. Bretthorst, "Bayesian Spectrum Analysis and Parameter Estimation" (1988)**
- Application of Jaynes' methods — shows the architecture in use by a student
- Tests cognitive inheritance (like Hermann from Noether)
