These failures demarcate the boundaries of formal decomposition when applied to discovery driven by aesthetic and tacit variables.

By documenting the systemic inability of the protocol to reduce P.A.M. Dirac’s breakthroughs into actionable steps, we reveal the hard limits of algorithmic knowledge capture. This analysis begins with the primary failure mode: the irreconcilable gap between the subject’s internal visualizations and his formal output.

2. The Geometry-Algebra Chasm: The "Suppressed Drawing" Problem

The strategic difficulty in extracting Dirac’s methodology stems from a fundamental methodological bifurcation between his internal representation and his external formalism. While Dirac’s public legacy is that of a "brilliant algebraist" whose equations exhibited crystalline logic, his cognitive process relied on tacit knowledge that was essentially geometrical. As analyzed in Peter Galison’s "The Suppressed Drawing," the subject demonstrated a unique cognitive duality: he "published on one side and worked on the other." This bifurcation represents a major point of friction for the PASS Ω protocol, which is designed to extract verbalized heuristics, not "hidden" geometric models that the subject intentionally suppressed.

This friction reached a limit during Dirac’s 1972 talk at Boston University. When the moderator, Roger Penrose, attempted to bridge the gap by asking how projective geometry specifically influenced quantum mechanics, Dirac’s response was his "trademark shake of the head" and a direct refusal to verbalize the link. The protocol identifies this as a lack of a translatable interface; the 2D projective geometry used in his private heuristics has no accessible semantic mapping to the N-dimensional quantum algebraic space presented in his papers.

3. The "Beauty" Variable: Resistance to Operator Decomposition

Mathematical beauty, for Dirac, was a predictive heuristic that functioned as a higher-order requirement than experimental agreement. The PASS Ω protocol struggles with this variable because aesthetic reasoning is fundamentally non-utilitarian. Dirac famously followed the example of William Rowan Hamilton, admiring his pursuit of "pretty mathematics" despite its lack of physical evidence for a century. To Dirac, the standard protocol variable of "Empirical Validation" was secondary; if a theory was beautiful, he insisted one should not be "too depressed" by observational disagreement.

The protocol's attempt to decompose this aesthetic filter reveals three reasons why "Beauty" resists standard "if-then" operator mapping:

1. Non-Linear Temporal Horizons: Dirac’s decision-making ignored the standard feedback loops of utility. He favored lines of work where the "success" might remain hidden for 100 years, rendering "Consensus Utility" an invalid metric for real-time extraction.
2. Inversion of Data Hierarchy: In instances like the initial failure of the Schrödinger equation to account for hydrogen's fine structure (due to spin), Dirac’s heuristic would have been to maintain the "beautiful" equation and reject the "ugly" data—a move the current protocol flags as a logical error.
3. Aesthetic Irreducibility: "Beauty" functions as a top-level, irreducible operator. It is not a secondary sentiment but the primary filter through which all logic must pass. It cannot be decomposed because it is the fundamental "Final Answer" standard the subject used to judge the very laws of nature.

This reliance on an internal aesthetic filter is intrinsically linked to the subject's reliance on non-active synthesis during periods of unstructured time.

4. Subconscious Synthesis vs. Procedural Study

The extraction protocol is effective at documenting "procedural" study—such as Dirac’s self-directed mastery of Whittaker’s Analytical Dynamics. However, it fails significantly when accounting for the "Sunday Walk" breakthrough of 1925. The transition from the intensive procedural study of Heisenberg’s matrices to the discovery of the similarity between the commutator (uv - vu) and the Poisson bracket was achieved through "relaxation-based" synthesis.

While the protocol is optimized for "active operators," Dirac’s most vital synthesis occurred when his work was kept only "faintly in the back of my mind" during a country walk. The subsequent "uncertain night" Dirac spent waiting for libraries to open highlights a critical Memory/Reference Dependency Failure. Because he could not remember the precise definition of the Poisson bracket from Whittaker’s book, the breakthrough was suspended in an unverified state. A protocol focused on procedural steps cannot weigh these "dead time" incubation periods as generative, yet for Dirac, the relaxation was the catalyst that allowed the subconscious to map algebraic matrix structures onto classical dynamics.

5. Anomaly Management: The Rejection of Successful Formalisms

The final failure mode for the protocol involves the strategic challenge of "founder repudiation." In his later career, the subject demonstrated a total repudiation of utilitarian variables, rejecting theories that the broader physics community considered essential. This highlights a protocol paradox: the extraction subject rejects the "successes" of his own field.

Dirac dismissed Renormalization as "nonsense" because the necessity of "infinitely great factors" violated his aesthetic standard of a final answer; he dismissed the experimental success as a mere "fluke." Similarly, he viewed his initial acceptance of the Bohr Orbit Theory as a "mistake" that gave him "too narrow an outlook." His 1951 insistence on reintroducing the Aether—driven by a need for the "unity of the metric system of the universe"—further proves that his methodology was based on a "Final Answer" standard that the protocol, and the modern community, has abandoned in favor of complacent utilitarianism.
