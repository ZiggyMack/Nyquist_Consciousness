# Operator 013: Epistemic Boundary Setting

**Confidence:** YELLOW

**Independent confirmations:** 1

**Class:** Information Operator

---

## Definition

Explicitly declare what you don't know before claiming what you do — draw the boundary between the known and the unknown, and resist the temptation to fill the unknown with speculation.

---

## The Operation

Before making a knowledge claim, first declare: (1) what the formalism determines, (2) what it does not determine, (3) what would be needed to determine the rest. The boundary itself is informative — knowing the shape of your ignorance constrains what answers are possible.

Barandes does this with ISP's "aesthetic gap": he acknowledges that ISP doesn't specify joint probabilities or reveal trajectories, and he explicitly says these are open questions rather than claiming the gap doesn't matter or will be filled later.

**Detection condition:** When a thinker makes a strong positive claim. Check: did they declare what remains unknown? If not, the claim may be overstepping what the evidence supports.

**Scope:** Universal — applicable to any knowledge-producing activity.

---

## Examples

### From Barandes (Dig Site 002)

ISP resolves the measurement problem and reproduces quantum axioms. But Barandes explicitly states: (1) ISP does not specify joint probabilities at non-division-event times, (2) ISP does not tell you what trajectory the system takes between division events, (3) these are genuine gaps, not features. He calls this an "aesthetic" gap — the theory works but doesn't tell you everything you might want to know. He does NOT claim the gap is irrelevant or will be filled soon.

---

## Failure Modes

1. **Boundary Erasure:** Claiming to know what you don't. The most common failure — filling the gap between what the formalism determines and what you claim with unstated assumptions. This is what Many-Worlds does with the Born rule (claims probability "emerges" from branch structure without a rigorous derivation).
2. **Boundary Inflation:** Declaring too much as unknown, paralyzing inquiry. "We can't know anything about X" when in fact partial knowledge is available. Barandes avoids this — he doesn't say ISP tells us nothing about trajectories; he says it tells us they exist but not what they are.
