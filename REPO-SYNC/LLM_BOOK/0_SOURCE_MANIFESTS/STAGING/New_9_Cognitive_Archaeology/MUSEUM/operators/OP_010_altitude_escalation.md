# Operator 010: Altitude Escalation

**Confidence:** YELLOW
**First recovered:** Dig Site 002 (Nova, meta-analysis of Barandes extraction)
**Independent confirmations:** 1 (demonstrated in action: Q1-Q20 → Q21-Q40 generation)
**Extractor-independence:** Unknown
**Class:** Information Operator

---

## Definition

Climb one meta-level to ask questions about the questions — shift from extracting content to extracting the cognitive architecture that produced the content.

---

## The Operation

When a set of questions or findings has been produced, do not merely continue at the same level. Instead, ask: "What kind of questions are these? What do they reveal about the structure of the inquiry itself? What questions would I need to ask to understand the PROCESS that generated these answers, not just the answers?"

The operation takes a completed level-N extraction and generates the level-(N+1) questions that would maximally increase understanding of the underlying reasoning architecture.

**Detection condition:** When a set of answers has been collected and the analyst notices diminishing returns — the answers are getting longer but the understanding isn't deepening. This signals that the current inquiry level is saturated and altitude escalation is needed.

**Scope:** Universal — applicable to any multi-round extraction or investigation. Not domain-conditional.

---

## Examples

### From Nova (Dig Site 002)

Q1-Q20 exhausted ISP content extraction (Levels 0-2: Facts, Mechanism, Architecture). Returns were diminishing. Nova applied Altitude Escalation to generate Q21-Q40, which targeted Levels 3-5 (Discovery, Transfer, Operating System). The escalation didn't produce "harder versions of Q1-Q20" — it produced a qualitatively different category of question. Q21 asks about compression, Q33 asks for the discovery operator itself, Q40 asks for a theory of scientific discovery. These are questions ABOUT the reasoning, not questions about the theory.

### The 6-Level Stack (discovered via this operator)

The operator itself revealed the inquiry stack it operates on:
- Level 0: Facts (what did they say?)
- Level 1: Mechanism (how does the theory work?)
- Level 2: Architecture (how is the theory organized?)
- Level 3: Discovery (how did they think?)
- Level 4: Transfer (where else would this operator work?)
- Level 5: Operating System (what would they contribute to an Epistemic Operating System?)

Q1-Q20 covered Levels 0-2. Altitude Escalation is the operation that jumps to Level 3+.

---

## Failure Modes

1. **Premature Escalation:** Climbing a level before the current level is saturated. If Q1-Q20 had not been answered, Q21-Q40 would be untethered speculation rather than grounded meta-inquiry. The lower level must be earned before escalation.
2. **Infinite Regress:** Escalating indefinitely — asking questions about questions about questions without ever grounding in content. The operator should stop when no genuinely new level emerges (the recursive meta-cycle has a natural termination condition).
3. **Level Confusion:** Mistaking a harder same-level question for a higher-level question. "What are division events?" (Level 1) vs "Why was this invisible for fifty years?" (Level 3) — these look similar but live at different altitudes.

---

## Counterexamples

### CE-1: Barandes — Staying at Ground Level

**Violation:** Barandes deliberately does NOT escalate in the interview. He stays at Levels 0-2 (the physics, the mechanism, the comparisons). He does not meta-analyze his own cognitive process.

**Did it work?** Yes — for his purpose. He's doing physics, not cognitive archaeology.

**What this tells us:** Altitude Escalation is an analyst's operator, not a practitioner's operator. The thinker doesn't need to escalate; the extractor does. This scopes the operator: it's a tool for studying thinkers, not a tool thinkers use on themselves.

## Alternative Decompositions

### AD-1: Altitude Escalation = Question Completion + Level Detection

**Decomposition:** Could be decomposed into two sub-operations: (1) detect that the current level is saturated, (2) generate questions at the next level. These might be separable operators.

**Evidence for:** Level detection (knowing WHEN to escalate) feels different from question generation (knowing WHAT to ask at the new level).

**Evidence against:** In practice, recognizing saturation and generating the next-level questions happen simultaneously — you can't separate "I've exhausted Level 2" from "and here's what Level 3 looks like."
