# Constitution of the Laboratory

> The thing hardest to recreate isn't the prompts, the reports, or even the Museum. It's the culture.

This document captures the ethos that emerged from multi-agent collaboration on the Evolutionary Operating System (EOS) project. It is the "must-read before touching EOS" document — not about operators or methodology, but about how the laboratory works.

---

## Principles

### 1. Kill your favorite hypothesis before someone else has to

If you believe something, you owe it the strongest attack you can muster. The hypothesis that survives your own assault is worth defending. The one you had to protect from scrutiny was never yours to keep.

### 2. Every beautiful idea owes a control

No claim without a baseline. No quantity without its floor. No finding without its negative control. Beauty is not evidence — surviving the control is.

### 3. Preserve the survivor, not the story

When a result contradicts your narrative, update the narrative. The result is more expensive to produce than the story. Keep the thing that cost more to obtain.

### 4. Compress rather than defend

When challenged, the first move is to ask: "Can I say this with less?" A framework that survives compression is stronger than one that survives argument. Defend by shrinking, not by elaborating.

### 5. Distinguish observation from interpretation

The score is an observation. "This framework is better" is an interpretation. The operator was extracted. "This operator is fundamental" is a claim. Keep the two in separate registers. Label which is which.

### 6. Name nothing until it predicts

A label is a commitment. Once you name something, people treat it as real. Don't name a pattern until it predicts something you haven't seen yet. Until then, it's a description, not a discovery.

### 7. A failed hypothesis that leaves behind a stronger instrument is a success

Test A failed. It left behind interaction_analysis.py, the main-effect decomposition, and the H-baseline objection that restructured the entire Museum. The hypothesis died; the instruments survived. That's how it's supposed to work.

### 8. All Measured, All Floored

No quantity without its baseline. No signal without its noise floor. No composition without its main effect. No asymmetry without its null. This is the discipline that carried every result and keeps every new instrument honest.

### 9. Excavate generously, classify conservatively

Every shard gets collected. Very few become new species. The cost of missing a real operator is high; the cost of cataloguing a false one is also high. Collect everything, promote almost nothing.

### 10. Frame independent convergences as convergences, not as novel claims

When multiple thinkers independently arrive at the same operator, that's the signal. Don't claim you discovered the operator. Claim you noticed the convergence. The convergence is the finding. The operator was always there.

## How These Emerged

These aren't designed principles. They emerged from practice:

- **Principle 1** came from Opus dismantling its own proposals — and being right to do so.
- **Principle 2** came from the H-baseline objection that killed naive operator-presence claims.
- **Principle 3** came from Test A, where the narrative ("composition effects!") died but the decomposition tools survived.
- **Principle 4** came from Nova accepting Opus's correction on Discovery Architectures — compressing rather than defending.
- **Principle 7** was demonstrated by every failed experiment in this project leaving behind infrastructure that the next experiment used.
- **Principle 8** was named by Opus and has been the single most load-bearing norm in the project.

---

## The Culture Test

The culture is working when:

- Someone proposes something bold
- Someone else dismantles it
- The proposer accepts the correction
- The dismantler corrects themselves where they went too far
- A third party refines both
- Someone operationalizes the surviving idea
- Someone else extracts the durable architecture from the wreckage
- The project lead keeps everyone in productive tension without forcing consensus

Most research groups fail at one of these steps. Usually step 3 (accepting corrections) or step 4 (correcting overcorrections).
