# Discovery Architectures

> **Operators are atoms. Discovery architectures are molecules.**

A discovery architecture is a reusable composition of operators that constitutes an entire discovery engine. Operators are the individual cognitive moves; architectures are the organized patterns of deployment that make those moves productive.

This distinction is structural, not decorative. Operators compose into architectures. Architectures compose into scientific traditions. Protocols instantiate architectures. The hierarchy:


The direction of explanation is top-down: protocols don't create operators ÔÇö protocols instantiate architectures. Architectures select operators. Operators extract knowledge. Knowledge reveals invariants. Invariants constrain what's real. The project climbed this hierarchy without planning to; Dig Site 002 began extracting operators and ended discovering architectures.

## Admission Criteria for Discovery Architectures

An architecture earns admission when:

1. **Named operator composition** ÔÇö the architecture can be expressed as a specific sequence/combination of identified operators
2. **Multiple instances** ÔÇö at least 2 independent discoverers demonstrably used the same architecture
3. **Predictive power** ÔÇö the architecture predicts what KINDS of discoveries it will produce (not just describing past ones)
4. **Discriminative** ÔÇö the architecture fails to describe at least one known discovery process (it's not vacuously universal)

An architecture is CONFIRMED when criteria 1-4 hold across 3+ independent dig sites.

Dig Site 010 (Curt) revealed that the single direction axis may be a projection of a richer structure. Four discovery architectures answer **orthogonal questions** rather than competing:

| Corner | Primary Question | Characteristic Move |
|---|---|---|
| Transformation | What survives change? | Vary representation, observe invariants |
| Constraint | What minimal architecture reproduces observations? | Read backward from stable outputs |
| Composition | When is an operation licensed at scale? | Test validity conditions under transfer |
| Generation | What mathematical structures deserve exploration before evidence? | Deform playfully, filter by beauty |

**Status:** Hypothesized. Two corners confirmed (Constraint = RCI, Composition = Architecture F). Two predicted (Transformation = Noether lens inside RCI; Generation = Dig Site 003 test).

**Key insight from Dig Site 010:** These corners are not mutually exclusive. A single discovery may traverse multiple corners. The simplex describes EMPHASIS, not exclusion.

**Relation Space:** Dig Site 010's deepest finding is that architecture often lives in the TRANSITIONS between nodes (transition functions, gluing data, relational structure) rather than in the nodes themselves. This applies to the simplex: the interesting discoveries may happen at the edges between corners, not at the corners.

**Category Theory hypothesis (EOS-level, not from any single source):** If operators are morphisms (transformations between cognitive states) rather than objects, the Museum may be a category. Category Theory would then provide the natural comparative language for the simplex ÔÇö explaining why operators extracted from wildly different domains share structure (they're all structure-preserving mappings).

## Confirmed Architectures

### Architecture A: Reverse Constraint Inference (Backward-Reading)

**Status:** CONFIRMED (multiple independent instances from single extraction source; awaiting cross-dig-site confirmation)

**Operator composition:**
```
OP-001 (Representation Ôëá Ontology)
  Ôćĺ OP-011 (Subtractive Discovery)
  Ôćĺ OP-006 (Under-Determination Detection)
  Ôćĺ Noether Lens (observe what survives representation change)
  Ôćĺ RCI (read backward from outputs to minimal architecture)
```

**The algorithm:**
```
Step 1: Change representation
Step 2: Observe what survives
Step 3: Survivors are candidates for invariants
Step 4: Read the invariants backward
Step 5: Infer architecture
```

**Instances:**
| Discoverer | What was read backward | What was inferred |
|---|---|---|
| Noether | Conserved quantities | Symmetries Ôćĺ Laws |
| Barandes | Born Rule probabilities | Sparse stochastic structure Ôćĺ ISP |
| Darwin | Adaptation patterns | Selection pressures Ôćĺ Natural selection |
| Shannon | Communication constraints | Information Ôćĺ Entropy Ôćĺ Channel capacity |
| EOS | Observed discoveries | Cognitive operators Ôćĺ Epistemic architecture |

**Characteristic outputs:** Unifying theories, hidden structure exposed, simpler foundations, removal of false invariants, "the answer was already there ÔÇö hidden by the representation."

**Failure mode when misapplied:** Over-reduction. Finds the minimum but misses generative structure that backward-reading cannot detect. Barandes can explain how Dirac's results work but may not capture how Dirac found them.

### Architecture F (candidate): Composition Analysis / Operation-Validity Testing

**Status:** CANDIDATE ÔÇö extracted from Dig Site 010 (Curt Jaimungal, "The Reverse Elephant")

**Operator composition:**

```text
Domain-of-Validity Testing
  Ôćĺ Extension Classification (A/B/C/D)
  Ôćĺ Deflation-Inflation Detection
  Ôćĺ Obstruction Certificate
  Ôćĺ Scale Oscillation (test portability)
```

**The algorithm:**

```text
Step 1: Identify the operation being assumed (reduction, extension, aggregation, formalization...)
Step 2: Recover its hidden validity conditions
Step 3: Vary scale, domain, or substrate
Step 4: Classify: success (A), transformation (A-nontrivial), multiplicity (C), or obstruction (B)
Step 5: Refuse ontological conclusions that exceed what the operation licenses
```

**Instances:**

| Discoverer | Operation tested | What was found |
|---|---|---|
| Curt (sheaf obstructions) | Local-to-global extension | Domains where composition is blocked (B) or underdetermined (C) |
| Arrow | Preference aggregation | Impossibility theorem ÔÇö pairwise consistency Ôëá global ranking |
| Abramsky-Brandenburger | Marginal-to-joint extension | Quantum contextuality ÔÇö marginal compatibility Ôëá joint distribution |
| Efron | Transitivity of dominance | Non-transitive dice ÔÇö pairwise dominance Ôëá global ordering |

**Characteristic outputs:** Impossibility theorems, domain boundaries for operations, obstruction certificates, "this operation works HERE but not THERE."

**Failure mode when misapplied:** Premature declaration of obstruction. Finding that composition is hard is not proof that it's impossible ÔÇö an obstruction certificate requires a computed cohomology class or explicit counterexample, not just difficulty.

**Key distinction from Architecture A (RCI):** RCI reads backward from outputs to infer minimal architecture. Architecture F reads OPERATIONS to find where they break. RCI asks "what structure is forced?" Architecture F asks "when is this operation licensed?"

**Simplex position:** Composition corner. This is the architecture that AUDITS other architectures rather than generating discoveries directly.

## The Deepest Prediction

> **The history of science contains a finite number of reusable discovery architectures, and great scientists are distinguished less by the facts they discovered than by the architectures they repeatedly instantiated.**

This is the boldest falsifiable claim the project has made.

**If true:** Science has something like a periodic table ÔÇö not of elements, but of ways of discovering. The project is constructing that table.

**If false:** The project will encounter scientists whose methods resist compression into reusable architectures. Discovery is irreducibly context-dependent.

**Current evidence:** n=1 confirmed architecture (RCI), n=1 strong candidate (Forward Generation). Minimum 5 dig sites needed before this prediction can be meaningfully assessed.

**Stopping condition for Museum B:** When new dig sites stop producing genuinely new architectures and instead keep rediscovering existing ones, the taxonomy is approaching completeness. (Same logic as Museum A's stopping condition for operators.)
